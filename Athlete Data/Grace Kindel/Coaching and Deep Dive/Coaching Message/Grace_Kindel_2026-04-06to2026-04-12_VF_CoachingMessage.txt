Grace,

Seventy straight Win days. Ten consecutive Growth weeks. You've now maintained your daily standard longer than any other athlete in this program, and you did it through a week where you didn't play a single minute.

Wednesday you sat in the dugout, didn't get called to warm up, and were still one of the loudest voices your teammates heard. Friday, same situation, same response: show up, stay positive, let the team feel your presence. That consistency isn't small. It takes more discipline to bring your full self to a game where you know you're not going in than to a game where you are. You're choosing to bring it anyway, and that choice says more about you than any at-bat result ever could.

You wrote something this week that I want you to hold onto: "If I get in, have fun. If I don't, have fun. Might as well enjoy where I'm at." That's not giving up. I know you know that, even though you said it's hard to explain. What you're describing is a decision to stop letting someone else's choices determine how you experience your season. You're still working, you're still hitting on your own Saturday, you're still winning every single day. The investment hasn't changed. What's changed is where you're putting the emotional weight, and that's a mature adjustment.

Here's what I want you to keep sharp. The acceptance you've found is real, and it's working for you right now. But acceptance works best when it sits next to something specific you're building. Saturday's hitting practice was that. The extra sessions from the last few weeks were that. Keep putting specific work next to the acceptance so it stays active, not just comfortable. "I don't care about playing time" and "I'm getting better at something specific today" can coexist, and when they do, you're in the strongest position you can be in regardless of what the lineup looks like.

YOUR ACTION: BEFORE EACH GAME THIS WEEK
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Before each game this week, write down one specific thing you want to bring to that day, whether you play or not. Not "be positive" or "have fun," but something you can actually do: own a specific warm-up drill, be the first one loud after a teammate's play, work on something specific in your own cage time. After the game, write one sentence naming what you brought. Three games, three intentions, three sentences.

You've already proven you show up every day. Now show yourself what showing up looks like when it's specific.

Coach Arron
