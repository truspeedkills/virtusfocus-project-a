Athlete Snapshot (Baseline Profile)

1.  Performance Context
Hunter Snyder is a high school wrestler with 10 years of
competitive experience. The intake was completed May 10, 2025
— during recovery from a season-ending knee injury sustained
in the Pennsylvania State Semi-Finals (PCL and LCL rupture,
surgery, active rehabilitation). He forfeited remaining
tournament matches and finished sixth in the state. He rates
his current mental game at 8/10 and identified 4 mental
struggle categories: Pressure, Fear of failure, Pre-game
nerves, and Staying consistent.

The competitive environment is individual sport at a state-
level competitive standard. Performance demands center on
high-stakes moments where mistakes are immediately visible
and cannot be distributed across a team. The injury context
adds a return-to-sport progression as a parallel preparation
demand alongside ordinary competitive readiness.

2.  Primary Stated Goals

Outcome Goals
- Be the best
- "Nothing but up"

Behavior Goals
- Build confidence
- Build belief
- Thrive under pressure

Long-term framing is championship-oriented ("I want to be the
best") paired with development goals centered on belief in
himself and composure under pressure. Stated hopes from the
program are "the ability to be great" and "the ability to
thrive under pressure" — coaching outcomes Hunter sees as
distinct from baseline competence.

3.  Motivation Drivers
Hunter's motivation is identity-driven and grounded in a
self-image as a top competitor. His pre-performance thought
pattern centers on accumulated preparation: "Think about the
amount of work I put in leading up to the moment" — an
approach-framed mental anchor that points to a healthy hinge
habit when activated.

Family support is high (parents rated very involved). No
current mindset coach or mentor. Commitment level is self-
reported as maximum ("All-in — let's go").

4.  Known Derailers
Four interconnected friction points:

- Pressure response in high-stakes moments
- Fear of failure expressed as outcome attachment ("the
  outcome of maybe losing")
- Pre-game nerves
- Staying consistent across competitive situations

Hunter's holding-back response names the central pattern:
when the pre-performance frame shifts from his work-anchored
process to outcome focus, fear-of-loss thinking takes over.
This is an approach-vs-avoidance tension, with the work-
anchored thought pattern as the approach side and "the
outcome of maybe losing" as the avoidance side. The 8/10
self-rating suggests this activates less frequently than for
program peers, but the pattern is named clearly.

5.  Confidence Pattern
Confidence is moderate-to-high in self-assessment (8/10) and
grounded in 10 years of accumulated wrestling experience. The
work-anchored pre-performance thought pattern indicates
confidence has a process foundation when activated correctly.
Listing "Confidence" and "Belief" as mindset goals signals
that baseline confidence is present but trust in himself
under pressure is a development edge, not a settled trait.

6.  Identity Friction
Hunter expresses a clear aspirational identity ("I want to be
the best") alongside named development gaps in confidence,
belief, and pressure response. The friction is between the
competitor he wants to be and the still-developing trust in
his own preparation under high-stakes conditions.

The injury layer adds a current-state friction: returning
from major knee surgery introduces a gap between past
competitive identity and present rehabilitation reality. His
framing of recovery — "Nothing but up" — positions the
process as forward trajectory rather than setback, but the
gap between past competitive standing and current physical
state is real.

  milestones, technical progress, accumulated reps as
  capability proof
- Establish consistency anchors that hold across pressure
  conditions, not just ordinary competition
- Frame recovery progression as competitive identity
  development, not a pause
