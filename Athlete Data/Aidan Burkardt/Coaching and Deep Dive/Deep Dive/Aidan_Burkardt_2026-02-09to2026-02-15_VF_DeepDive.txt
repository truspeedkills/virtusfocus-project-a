You wrote in your Bullseye that you can choose to overthink or just see the results for what they are and analyze them with others instead of by yourself. That sentence captures something most athletes never learn to put into words — that the moment after a disappointing result contains a choice, and that choice is yours. You named the specific alternative: collaborative analysis instead of isolated fixation. That level of awareness, in the same week the disappointment happened, is the most significant reflection you have produced in four weeks.

*You recognized the fork in the road while you were standing at it — not after you had already gone down the wrong path. That is a skill that compounds every time you use it.*

ANALYTICAL COMPETITION PROCESSING
After the 60m, you did not just react to the time. You broke the race into segments — looked at your 30-60 split, evaluated your top-end speed, and identified your start as the specific limiting factor. You did the same for the 200m, comparing your training 150 times to what showed up in the race and searching for where the translation gap lives. That is an athlete choosing to understand his performance rather than just feeling the number.

*The ability to dissect a race into actionable pieces — rather than letting the final time become the whole story — is what separates athletes who improve from athletes who repeat.*

CONTROLLABLE FOCUS UNDER COMPETITIVE PRESSURE
Your Bullseye this week was the most detailed you have produced in the program. Four distinct controllables: present-moment focus, the choice between overthinking and analysis, channeling disappointment into productive energy, and naming confidence itself as something you control. After results that did not cooperate, you kept your attention on what you can actually influence rather than drifting into what the clock showed.

*Most athletes lose sight of their controllables when results disappoint. You identified more — and more specific ones — than in any previous week.*

PREPARATION AND RECOVERY DISCIPLINE

Then Friday and Saturday you deliberately pulled back ahead of the meet. That intentional shift from training to recovery shows you understand rest before competition is part of the plan, not a break from it.

*Training hard all week and then trusting the recovery process before the meet — that is a mature approach to preparation.*

GROWTH AREAS
------------------------------------------------------------

EXPECTATION-TO-EVALUATION GAP
You went into this meet with specific targets — 7.2 in the 60m, sub-23 in the 200m. When the results came back at 7.5 and 23.4, the emotional response was immediate. You described it as demoralizing. The pattern underneath is one you know well: measuring results against where you believe you should be based on years of training rather than against where you actually are right now in your development. The confidence you built through Weeks 1-3 was genuine, but it was built on preparation — and competition introduces a different kind of test, one where the evaluation system matters just as much as the preparation itself.

*There is a difference between confidence built on what you have done in training and confidence that holds when the clock shows a number you did not want. This week showed the first kind is strong. Building the second is the next layer.*

You named this one yourself — the pull toward analyzing results alone rather than with others. The fact that you identified collaborative analysis as the healthier path is a real strength. But naming it also tells you something about where your default setting still sits. When the disappointment hit, the first instinct was to internalize. You caught it and described the alternative clearly. The default is still there, though, and it is worth paying attention to how often you find yourself processing alone versus reaching out.

*Knowing your default and choosing differently are two separate stages. You are at the stage where you can see it clearly — which is exactly where the shift starts.*

WEEKEND SCORING CONSISTENCY
Across all four weeks, your weekends consistently go unscored. Weekday execution is strong and well-documented, but Saturdays and Sundays drop off the tracking even when you are productive. This week the unscored days were deliberate recovery — but the broader pattern shows that unstructured days get less engagement with the system.

*The days without external structure often reveal the most about where your habits stand on their own.*
