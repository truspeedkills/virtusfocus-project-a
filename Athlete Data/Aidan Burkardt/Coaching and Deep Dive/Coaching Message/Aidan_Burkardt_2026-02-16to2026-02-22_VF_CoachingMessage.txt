Aidan —

You asked me a direct question this week, so I'm going to answer it directly.

You noticed that you're mentally rehearsing your start and race feelings throughout the day — checking to make sure they're still there. And you recognized that the constant rehearsal is draining you before practice. That observation alone is the kind of self-awareness most athletes never develop.

Here's the answer: visualization works when it has a window. It stops working when it runs all day.

Limit your deliberate visualization to one specific time — the five minutes before your block or speed session begins. That's your window. Before the session, you see it, you feel it, you commit to it. Then you execute.

Outside that window, when you catch yourself rehearsing starts or replaying race feelings during the day, name it: "I'm checking again." Then let it pass. The training is already in your body. You don't need to verify that it's still there. It is.

The rest of your week tells the story of why you can trust that. Recovery after the meet. Block session Wednesday to reorganize mentally. Speed session Thursday where you held near 10 meters per second on a bad track. Max effort upper with a PR Saturday. Special endurance 2x150 at 16.8 and 16.4. The work is real. The preparation is real. The execution shows up when you let it — not when you chase it through your thoughts all day.

Your Bullseye this week was the most advanced in your program. "I can control if I will let a time define who I identify as." "I can control letting go of what my mind thinks I should be thinking about to validate myself." Those aren't just controllables. Those are identity-level decisions. You're choosing who you are, not waiting for a stopwatch to tell you.

Your achievement statement said it: "a stronger mental approach by being comfortable with letting go." You named a mental skill as your week's primary accomplishment — not a PR, not a time, not a training number. That shift matters.
