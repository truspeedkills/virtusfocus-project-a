SECTION 1: EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
============================================================

The v2.3/V5 architecture produced a measurable improvement
in voice quality. Week 8 output is the cleanest in the set
across all 5 analysis dimensions: zero Criterion 14
violations, zero em dashes, zero retired constructions, zero
pipeline classification language in CMs, and the widest
expressive range of any week analyzed. The improvement is not
attributable to "better model output" generically -- it maps
directly to specific architectural decisions: the Voice
Reference teaching through exemplars rather than prohibiting
through rules, the v2.3 Section 35 retirement-only
architecture eliminating workaround behavior, the removal of
legacy brand files eliminating structural templates, and
Criterion 14 providing a post-generation quality gate. The
hypothesis that teaching produces better voice than
prohibition is supported by this first data point. Week 7
(v2.1) represented a meaningful intermediate improvement over
Weeks 3/5 (v1.8), confirming that the voice quality work was
progressive, but Week 8 clears the remaining v2.1 gaps
(telegraphing, pipeline language leakage, DD structural
variety).

P0: SERVICE FIRST
------------------------------------------------------------
Week 8 demonstration: The entire CM is built around what
Angelo needs going into Slippery Rock Thursday. The weather
lesson, the practice instability acknowledgment, and the
honest prior-commitment gap assessment all serve the
athlete's immediate coaching need.

Best prior: Week 7 CM is also strongly service-oriented,

Worst prior: Week 5 DD drifts into reporting mode with
pipeline metrics ("rolling win rate 89 percent," "consistency
indicators re-enter the positive band") that serve data
completeness, not the athlete.

P1: START WITH MEANING, NOT DESCRIPTION
------------------------------------------------------------
Week 8 CM: "Friday's weather hit you with something you
didn't prepare for." Opens with what happened and immediately
moves to what Angelo DID about it. The meaning (two kinds of
toughness) emerges from the narrative rather than being
stated as a topic sentence.

Week 8 DD: "This was the first week in your program where
things didn't go the way you planned." Opens with the
meaning of the week (disruption after 7 steady weeks), not a
description of events.

Best prior: Week 7 CM opens with the athlete's own
meaning-laden language ("Greater mental stability and
equilibrium"). Strong P1 alignment.

Worst prior: Week 5 CM opens stat-led ("Best outdoor season
opener of your career"). While the stat IS the story, the
opening describes what happened rather than leading with why
it matters. Week 5 DD is worse: "[N] weeks into this
program" is a temporal anchor, not meaning.

P2: SPEND YOUR WORDS UNEVENLY
------------------------------------------------------------
Week 8 CM: The weather/competition story gets the most space
(paragraph 2, longest). Practice instability gets direct
treatment (paragraph 3). Prior commitment gap gets brief,
honest handling (paragraph 4, shortest). Clear proportional
weighting by importance.

Week 8 DD: The Trendline section is the longest and deepest,
connecting PSAC and Bullseye threads at length because those
are the two historically relevant patterns. Growth Areas
gives disproportionate depth to "Competition-Day Mental
Preparation" over "Practice Instability." Strong proportional
weighting.

Best prior: Week 7 DD is also proportionally weighted.

Worst prior: Weeks 3 and 5 DDs each have 4 strength signals
with roughly equal space. Coverage feels report-like rather
than proportional to importance.

P3: TALK LIKE YOU'VE BEEN IN THE ROOM
------------------------------------------------------------
Week 8 CM: "Rain, cold, wind, no extra clothes, and a
forecast that lied to you." Vivid, specific, lists the
sensory details of the experience. "I'm not going to pretend
those numbers don't matter to you." Direct, human. "You
called these 'horrible circumstances,' and that's fair. They

Week 8 DD: "I'm not going to tell you to just focus on what

Best prior: Week 7 CM: "Look at that list and compare it to

Worst prior: Week 5 DD: "That initiative is what separates

"That is leadership behavior." Declarative label rather than

P4: EARN EVERY SENTENCE
------------------------------------------------------------
Week 8 CM: Every sentence carries specific information or
does coaching work. "I'm not going to pretend those numbers

Week 8 DD: "The preparation side took a hit. You trusted the
forecast, didn't pack for weather, and paid for it on

Three sentences, each carrying specific information.

Worst prior: Week 3 DD: "That is leadership behavior." A
declarative stamp that announces rather than demonstrates.
Week 5 DD: "The athletes who create their own development
moments are the ones who get ahead of their competition
calendar." Generic principle that doesn't earn its space with
this-athlete specificity. "Process-focused athletes do not

P5: REACT, DON'T COMPOSE
------------------------------------------------------------
Week 8 CM: Clearly reactive. Opens with the weather story
because that's what defined the week. Moves to practice
instability because Angelo named it. Addresses the prior
commitment because it's honest coaching. Ends with Slippery
Rock because that's next. The structure follows the week, not

Week 8 DD: The trendline section connects PSAC and Bullseye

Worst prior: Weeks 3 and 5 DDs have 4 strength signals each

============================================================

OPENING APPROACHES:
  Wk3 CM: Quality judgment ("This was your best week yet")
  Wk5 CM: Stat-led achievement ("Best outdoor season opener")
  Wk7 CM: Athlete quote ("Greater mental stability...")
  Wk8 CM: Specific moment ("Friday's weather hit you")

  Wk3 DD: Identity statement ("preparation became identity")
  Wk5 DD: Temporal anchor ("[N] weeks into this program")
  Wk7 DD: Week character ("a week about proving you can rest")
  Wk8 DD: Defining contrast ("first week where things didn't
          go the way you planned")

Assessment: Four different CM opening approaches across 4
weeks. The progression moves from external description
(Wk3/5 -- what happened to you) toward internal reaction
(Wk7/8 -- what you did about it). Week 8's opening is the
most reactive and specific.

SENTENCE RHYTHM:
  Weeks 3/5: Predominantly long sentences. Formal academic
  feel with little rhythmic variation. Sentences tend toward
  compound structures with semicolons and subordinate clauses.

  Week 7: More varied. Mix of long analytical sentences and
  shorter declaratives. "Not a throw distance. Not a
  placement." introduces fragment rhythm.

  Week 8 CM: Strong rhythmic variation across lengths:
  "Rain, cold, wind, no extra clothes, and a forecast that
  lied to you." (list rhythm)
  "You competed through it anyway." (short declarative)
  "Saturday you brought the gear even though the sun was
  out." (medium narrative)
  "That's two different kinds of toughness in the same
  weekend, and the second one tells me more than the first."
  (long analytical)

  This is the widest sentence-length range in the set.

RECOGNITION MOVES:
  Wk3: Labels behavior ("That is what intentional preparation
  looks like at a high level")
  Wk5: Elevates athlete's words ("That sentence is a coaching
  philosophy, not just a recap")
  Wk7: Invites self-recognition ("Look at that list and
  compare it to what you told me at intake")
  Wk8: Comparative recognition ("That's two different kinds
  of toughness in the same weekend, and the second one tells
  me more than the first") -- distinguishes between types of
  strength, names which one matters more. NEW move.
  Wk8: Directional recognition ("Your frustration about the
  practice instability is pointed outward at the conditions,
  not inward at yourself") -- recognizes the direction of
  frustration as the signal. Another NEW move.

CLOSING MOVES:
  Wk3 CM: Short anchor ("Stay grounded")
  Wk5 CM: Generic ("Keep building")
  Wk7 CM: Callback ("Planned, not spontaneous. That's your
  standard now.")
  Wk8 CM: Grounded callback ("Prepared for anything. That's
  the standard you're building.")
  Wk8 DD: Direct challenge with timeline ("Slippery Rock is
  Thursday. Five days. You know what the preparation gap was
  this weekend. Close it before you step into the ring.")

  The Week 8 DD close is a genuinely new move that doesn't
  appear in any prior week -- a forward-facing challenge
  grounded in this week's specific gap, with a deadline.

OVERALL EXPRESSIVE RANGE ASSESSMENT:
Week 8 demonstrates expanded range, not just cleaner
execution of the same patterns. The recognition moves are
genuinely new (comparative, directional). The sentence rhythm

SECTION 7: ARCHITECTURAL ATTRIBUTION
============================================================

Each observed improvement mapped to its source:

1. OPENING SPECIFICITY (Wk8 vs Wk3/5)
   Source: Voice Reference P1 (Start with Meaning) + P5
   (React, Don't Compose) + Category A exemplars.
   The Voice Reference teaches opening with a specific moment,
   and v2.3 promotes this through exemplars rather than
   prohibiting stat-led openings through rules. Week 7 (v2.1)
   already showed improvement here (athlete quote opening),
   but v2.1 used a rule-based approach ("opening approach
   variety"). v2.3's exemplar-teaching approach produced a
   further step forward.

2. TELEGRAPHING ELIMINATION (Wk8 vs all 3 prior)
   Source: Voice Reference P4 (Earn Every Sentence) + Category
   C exemplars + Criterion 14.2 (post-generation detection).
   Critical finding: v2.1 (Week 7) still had "Now here's
   where I want to push you" despite having an explicit
   no-telegraphing rule. v2.3 (Week 8) has zero instances.
   This supports the hypothesis that teaching through
   exemplars (showing what direct coaching looks like) is
   more effective than prohibition (telling the model not to
   telegraph). The model learned from Category C exemplars
   that a coach doesn't announce -- they just say it.

3. PIPELINE LANGUAGE ELIMINATION (Wk8 vs Wk3/5/7)
   Source: v2.3 Section 35 C4 (Pipeline Classification
   Language Prohibition) + Voice Reference P3 (Talk Like
   You've Been in the Room) + Purpose-Built Principle
   (removal of legacy brand files).
   Progressive improvement: Wk5 had 6+ instances, Wk7 had 2,
   Wk8 has 0 (with borderline "Win days" / "Gold Streaks"
   passing audit). The combined effect of prohibition (C4,
   present since v2.1) AND teaching (Voice Reference replacing
   pipeline language with behavioral description, v2.3 only)
   AND legacy file removal (Purpose-Built Principle removing
   content_style_guide.txt and brand_voice.txt which had no
   equivalent prohibition) produced the full elimination.

4. RETIRED C3 CONSTRUCTIONS (Wk8 vs Wk5)
   Source: v2.3 Section 35 C3 (retirement-only architecture).
   Direct attribution. "The question is [whether]" and "The
   athletes who [X] are the ones who [Y]" appeared in Week 5
   DD (v1.8, where these were not yet identified as problems).
   Week 8 uses neither. The retirement-only approach (no
   quota, no workaround path, just banned) works because there
   is nothing to game.

5. EM DASH ELIMINATION (Wk7/8 vs Wk3/5)
   Source: v2.1 Section 35 C1 (em dash ban, established
   Session 92). Already working in v2.1. v2.3 inherited
   this unchanged. Not a v2.3-specific improvement.

6. CONTRACTION REGISTER (Wk7/8 vs Wk3/5)
   Source: v2.1 Section 35 C2 (contraction default). Already
   working in v2.1. v2.3 inherited this unchanged. Not a
   v2.3-specific improvement.

7. "THAT IS [NOUN]" ELIMINATION (Wk7/8 vs Wk3)
   Source: v2.1 capped this pattern. v2.3 moved detection to
   Criterion 14.8 + Voice Reference Category B exemplars
   teach alternatives. Already working in v2.1 but the Voice
   Reference exemplars provide the model with concrete
   alternatives, reinforcing the elimination.

8. UNEVEN COVERAGE (Wk8 vs Wk3/5)
   Source: v2.3 Section 36 (Uneven Coverage Principle) + Voice
   Reference P2 (Spend Your Words Unevenly).
   v2.3-specific improvement. Weeks 3/5 DDs had 4 equally-
   weighted strength signals. Week 8 DD has proportional
   depth. This is a new architectural element that had no
   equivalent in v2.1.

9. DD DISTINCTNESS FROM CM (Wk8 vs Wk3/5)

   v2.3-specific improvement. Week 8 CM focuses on weather

10. "REFUSE TO COACH BADLY" MOVES (Wk8 only)

SECTION 8: REMAINING GAPS
============================================================

1. "WIN DAYS" / "GOLD STREAKS" BORDERLINE LANGUAGE
   Week 8 DD uses "Three Win days before the meet" (line 36)
   and "two Gold Streaks" (line 8). These passed the editorial
   audit under the reasonable reader test, and "Win days" is
   arguably natural language for "days you won." But these
   terms originate as pipeline categories ("Win" = 4-5 on
   WTD scoring), and a real coach would more likely say
   "three strong days" or "your second perfect week." This is
   a borderline area that deserves monitoring across athletes.
   Week 7 CM also used "Seven Win days. Gold Streak." under
   v2.1. The pattern persists into v2.3.

2. "THIS WAS THE [X] WEEK" DD OPENING TEMPLATE
   Week 8 DD opens "This was the first week in your program
   where things didn't go the way you planned." Week 7 DD
   opens "This was a week about proving you can rest." Week 3
   DD opens "This was the week your preparation became your
   identity." Three of four DDs use the "This was..." frame.
   If future output continues this pattern, it could become a
   14.1 reusable opening at the DD level. Not yet a violation
   (each "This was..." sentence is specific to its week) but
   an emerging template.

3. DD SUBSECTION STRUCTURAL TEMPLATE
   All 4 weeks' DDs use the same internal structure: section
   heading + paragraph(s) + sometimes italicized observation

   This analysis covers one athlete across 4 weeks. The

   confirmed by a single data point. As more athletes submit
   new weekly data under v2.3/V5, run the same Criterion 14
   pattern check on the first CM + DD for each athlete.

   calibration. If they persist across multiple athletes under
   v2.3, a targeted prohibition may be warranted. If they
   appear only when genuinely describing the athlete's program
   history (not as classification labels), the current audit
   judgment may be correct.

3. CONSIDER DD STRUCTURAL VARIATION GUIDANCE
   The Voice Reference teaches voice quality through
   exemplars but does not specifically address DD subsection
   format. The 18 exemplars are all paragraph-form passages,
   not DD-structured sections. If DD structural monotony
   persists across athletes under v2.3, consider adding a
   brief note to the Deep Dive Framework or Voice Reference
   about varying the heading/paragraph/capper format -- e.g.,
   sometimes opening a section with the observation rather
   than the description, or omitting the italic capper when
   the final paragraph already carries the teaching.

4. WATCH FOR V2.3-SPECIFIC TEMPLATE EMERGENCE
   v2.1 had its own template patterns (telegraphing, pipeline
   language, retired constructions). v2.3 may develop its own.

   trendline opener is effective but could become its own
   template if it appears in every DD.

5. NO SPEC CHANGES RECOMMENDED FROM THIS ANALYSIS
   This is one data point. The v2.3 architecture is producing
   the expected improvements. No specification changes are
   warranted until the sample size supports a pattern-level
   finding rather than a single-instance observation. The
   remaining gaps identified above are monitoring items, not
   action items.

APPENDIX B SECTION 5: "DEVELOPING PHASE" JUDGMENT CALL
============================================================

Week 9 DD uses "Developing phase" twice in the Identity
Thread section:

  "You're still in the Developing phase, and that isn't
   going to change on a single week."

  "The Developing phase is where you're building the
   explicit system the next phase will rely on."

The editorial audit judged both uses as PASS under 14.12,
noting: "'Developing phase' used naturally per DD Framework
permitted phrasing."

ANALYSIS:

C4 in v2.3 Section 35 lists as prohibited:
  "[Growth/Developing/Consistent] phase maintained"
Replacement: "Describe sustained behavior"

The C4 example is specifically about PHASE MAINTAINED as
a classification-state phrase. Week 9's usage is different:
the phase name is referenced as context for where the
athlete is in development, not as a classification status
label.

This is the same judgment pattern the audit has been
applying to "Win days" / "Gold Streaks" across the v2.3
corpus: the term passes when used descriptively (describing
what happened or where the athlete is), fails when used as
a pipeline classification label ("phase maintained," "Growth
weeks" as category).

CONSISTENCY CHECK ACROSS THE V2.3 CORPUS:
  - Angelo Wk8: no named phases in final output
  - Tucker Wk8: no named phases in final output
  - Grace Wk11: no named phases in final output
  - Angelo Wk9: "Developing phase" twice, judged acceptable

Angelo Wk9 is the first v2.3 output to use the phase name
in the final version. The audit judgment is defensible but
represents a boundary case. If subsequent output uses phase
names at increasing frequency, the "descriptive vs
classification" judgment will need tighter criteria.

FINDING: The phrase "still in the Developing phase" reads
as coaching context to an experienced reader but could
read as pipeline-classification to an expert reviewer
looking for AI tells. This is exactly the kind of borderline
that matters at the 95%-to-98% quality transition.
Recommendation: no spec change yet. Watch for the pattern
across the next 3 athlete weeks. If phase names appear in
50%+ of v2.3 output going forward, tighten the 14.12 check
to exclude phase names in all contexts.

         DECIDED IT."
  "You chose recovery when a lift wasn't available. You
  chose technique over volume. You listened before you
  acted. None of that happened by accident. You decided
  it."

  Parallel-affirmative construction (three "You chose"
  sentences + summation). This is structurally parallel,
  but it's parallel AFFIRMATIVE rather than parallel
  NEGATIVE (which would be the retired "Not X, Y" pattern).
  Not a violation. But: if this construction recurs at high
  frequency, it could become its own template. Monitor.

MOVE 2: "REFUSE THE OBVIOUS FRAMING" OPENER
  "Slippery Rock isn't a better result because anything
  around you got easier. [Three specific counter-evidence
  items.] What changed was you."

  This is a coaching move that sets up a false explanation,
  refutes it with specifics, and pivots to the real answer.
  Effective. But similar-shaped openings in subsequent
  weeks would be a template. Monitor.

MOVE 3: "THAT SENTENCE IS WHAT PRODUCED [OUTCOME]" AS
         RECOGNITION
  "That sentence is what produced the outdoor shot put
  school record over 16m."

  Rather than recognizing the athlete's behavior directly
  ("You did X well"), this recognition traces the outcome
  backward to a specific athlete utterance. The sentence
  becomes the causal agent. Strong P3 + P1 fusion.
