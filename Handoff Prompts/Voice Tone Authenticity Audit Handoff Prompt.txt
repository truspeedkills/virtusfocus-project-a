5. WHAT "AUTHENTICITY" MEANS IN THIS AUDIT
============================================================

An authentic coaching message sounds like a real human
coach sat down, reviewed the athlete's week, and wrote
a personal note. It should feel like a conversation
between Coach Arron and the athlete — not like a report
generated from structured data.

The pipeline IS generating from structured data (the
Interpretation JSON). That is by design. The question is
whether the output successfully hides the machinery and
reads as genuine human coaching communication.

THE CORE QUESTION FOR EVERY FILE:
"If this athlete showed this message to a teammate and
said 'my coach wrote this,' would the teammate believe
it was written by a human coach?"

6. SPECIFIC AI-PATTERN DETECTION CRITERIA
============================================================

Audit every file against the following categories. For each
category, provide specific examples (quote the exact text,
cite the file) and rate severity: MINOR (stylistic
preference), MODERATE (noticeable to careful reader),
MAJOR (obviously AI-generated to a reasonable reader).

------------------------------------------------------------
CATEGORY A: SENTENCE STRUCTURE PATTERNS
------------------------------------------------------------

A1. PARALLEL CONSTRUCTION OVERUSE
AI models tend to produce parallel sentence structures
excessively — same rhythm, same length, same grammatical
pattern repeated in sequence.

  EXAMPLE OF THE PROBLEM:
  "You showed up Monday. You pushed through Tuesday.
   You adapted Wednesday. You recovered Thursday."

  A real coach would vary the rhythm:
  "Monday you showed up. The Tuesday session was where
   the push-through happened — and by Wednesday you had
   already adapted your approach."

Look for: Consecutive sentences with identical grammatical
structure, especially in lists of behavioral observations.

A2. FORMULAIC TRANSITIONS
AI models use predictable transition patterns between
ideas. Look for:
- "Here is what..." / "Here is the thing..."
- "That matters because..."
- "This is worth naming..."
- "Now the test..."
- "The question is..."
- "What [X] tells me is..."

These are not inherently wrong — a real coach might use
any of them. The problem is frequency and predictability.
If every CM uses "Here is what I want you to see" as the
second-paragraph opener, it becomes a template, not a
conversation.

A3. TOPIC-SENTENCE-THEN-EVIDENCE PATTERN
AI models tend to state a conclusion and then support it.
Real coaching conversations are messier — sometimes the
evidence comes first and the insight emerges.

  AI PATTERN:
  "The biggest signal this week is the Bullseye expansion.
   For three weeks your center ring had one item..."

  HUMAN PATTERN:
  "For three straight weeks your center ring said the same
   thing. This week it opened up to six items — and that
   is worth stopping on."

Look for: Every paragraph opening with a thesis statement
followed by supporting evidence. Real coaches sometimes
lead with the detail and let the meaning emerge.

A4. CADENCE MONOTONY
AI tends to produce sentences of similar length. A real
coach varies between short punches and longer explanatory
sentences. Look for sections where every sentence is
15-25 words without variation.

A5. EXCESSIVE ENUMERATION
AI loves to list things in numbered or parallel form.

  AI PATTERN:
  "Three pattern shifts detected: (1) Bullseye expansion,
   (2) Gold Streak, (3) achievement language shift."

  This is fine in the JSON. It should not appear in
  athlete-facing coaching output. A coach would integrate
  these observations into narrative flow.

------------------------------------------------------------
CATEGORY B: VOCABULARY AND PHRASING PATTERNS
------------------------------------------------------------

B1. OVERUSED COACHING PHRASES
Track the frequency of specific phrases across all files.
Flag any phrase that appears in more than 30% of CMs or
DDs as potentially formulaic:
- "That matters"
- "That is worth naming"
- "Here is what I want you to see"
- "The question is"
- "This is not about [X] — it is about [Y]"
- "What [X] tells me is"
- "That is [noun]" as a standalone sentence (e.g., "That
  is advocacy." "That is ownership." "That is discipline.")
- "The [X] that produced [Y] has not been tested since [Z]"
- "Not [X] — [Y]" correction pattern

B2. REGISTER SHIFTS
Does the voice stay consistent within a single file? Look
for moments where the register shifts from conversational
coaching to analytical/clinical/report language.

  CONVERSATIONAL: "You lifted while sick. That tells me
   something about who you are in this program."

  ANALYTICAL SHIFT: "The recommitment behavior under
   adverse conditions demonstrates a strong engagement
   baseline that holds under physical adversity."

The second version is accurate but reads like a
performance review, not a coaching message.

B3. PIPELINE VOCABULARY LEAKAGE
Even when pipeline terms are not used explicitly (which
would be a compliance violation), their conceptual
fingerprints can leak into the coaching voice:

  LEAKAGE: "Your consistency profile held at Stable
   Positive" — uses the actual field name.

  SUBTLER LEAKAGE: "Your consistency has been stable and
   positive across the last four weeks" — mirrors the
   classification structure even though it uses natural
   language.

  AUTHENTIC: "Four straight Growth weeks now. That kind
   of run is not accidental."

B4. ADVERB AND QUALIFIER OVERUSE
AI models tend to use qualifiers excessively: "notably,"
"significantly," "genuinely," "particularly," "clearly,"
"directly." A real coach either commits to the statement
or does not make it.

  AI: "This is genuinely significant progress."
  COACH: "This is progress."

B5. HEDGING LANGUAGE
AI hedges to avoid overstatement. A confident coach does
not hedge:

  AI: "This may suggest that your preparation quality
   is improving."
  COACH: "Your preparation is improving."

B6. EM DASH OVERUSE
AI models (Claude in particular) tend to overuse em dashes
for parenthetical insertions. Count em dash frequency per
file. A real coach might use 1-3 per page. More than 5-6
in a 400-word CM suggests AI patterning.

------------------------------------------------------------
CATEGORY C: STRUCTURAL PATTERNS
------------------------------------------------------------

C1. OPENING HOOK FORMULA
Do all CMs open the same way? Track the opening pattern
across all files:
- Metric-first opening ("Seven for seven. Gold Streak.")
- Behavioral-first opening ("You showed up this week.")
- Context-first opening ("This was a week where...")
- Challenge-first opening ("I want to push you on...")

If 80%+ of CMs use the same opening pattern, the formula
is detectable regardless of content variation.

C2. PARAGRAPH FLOW PREDICTABILITY
Does every CM follow the same Recognition → Strength →
Drift → Identity → Action arc? The framework specifies
this arc, but a real coach would sometimes lead with the
challenge, sometimes lead with the question, sometimes
skip recognition entirely when the week demands it.

Flag any CM where the structure is so predictable that
you could label each paragraph by its role without
reading the content.

C3. DEEP DIVE SECTION SAMENESS
The DD framework mandates 5 sections. But within each
section, is there structural variation? Or does every
DD follow the exact same internal pattern:
- Mindset Summary: always opens with "[N] weeks into
  this program..." or "This was the week that..."
- Trendline: always opens with longitudinal metrics
- Strength Signals: always 3 items with same format
- Growth Areas: always 2-3 items with same format
- Identity Thread: always opens with intake reference

C4. YOUR ACTION SECTION VARIATION
Does the action section vary in format, specificity, and
voice? Or does every CM follow the same pattern of
[micro-commitment restated as instruction]?

C5. CLOSING PHRASE VARIETY
Track the closing phrase used in every CM across all
athletes. The framework specifies a set ("Stay centered,"
"Lead yourself first," "Control what's in your center
ring," "Progress over perfection") but a real coach would
also use contextual closings. How much variety exists?

------------------------------------------------------------
CATEGORY D: CROSS-ATHLETE CONSISTENCY vs. INDIVIDUATION
------------------------------------------------------------

D1. VOICE DIFFERENTIATION
A real coach adjusts their voice for different athletes.
Grace Kindel (freshman softball player, 9 weeks in) should
receive a different register than Jasmine Jones (Olympic
bobsled athlete, 7 weeks in) or John Tastinger
(entrepreneur, Week 1).

Read CMs from the same week across different athletes.
Does the voice actually shift? Or could you swap the
athlete names and the messages would still read the same?

Dimensions to check:
- Vocabulary complexity (should scale with athlete
  sophistication and competitive level)
- Directness/accountability level (should scale with
  athlete's program tenure and growth phase)
- Sport-specific language (should be natural and specific
  to each athlete's domain)
- Emotional calibration (should match the athlete's
  emotional intensity classification)

D2. COACHING RELATIONSHIP MATURITY
Compare Week 1 CMs to Week 7+ CMs for the same athlete.
Does the coaching voice mature over time? A real coach
builds familiarity — the tone should shift from
introductory to established. Look for:
- Week 1: More orientation, more explanation, more
  encouragement
- Week 7+: More shorthand, more direct challenge, more
  expectation of athlete understanding

D3. WEEK-TYPE DIFFERENTIATION
A Gold Streak week should feel different from a first
Missed day week. A competition week should feel different
from a recovery week. A High EI week should feel different
from a Low EI week.

Read one Gold Streak CM and one disrupted-week CM for the
same athlete. Does the voice actually shift in energy,
rhythm, directness, and focus? Or is the same coaching
template applied regardless of week character?

------------------------------------------------------------
CATEGORY E: DEEP DIVE SPECIFIC ISSUES
------------------------------------------------------------

E1. ITALICIZED OBSERVATION QUALITY
The DD framework specifies italicized coaching
observations at the end of each Strength Signal and
Growth Area. These are supposed to be insight — the "so
what" that a real coach would say after laying out the
evidence.

Audit whether these observations:
- Add genuine insight (not just restating the evidence)
- Vary in style and structure across files
- Sound like a coach reflecting, not an AI summarizing
- Avoid formulaic framing ("The takeaway here is...")

E2. TRENDLINE SECTION NATURALNESS
The Trendline section contains longitudinal data. The
challenge is presenting this data in a coaching voice
rather than a data-analysis voice.

  DATA-ANALYSIS: "Rolling win rate across the last four
   weeks stands at 96.4 percent. Missed rate at 3.6
   percent. Volatility index Low."

  COACHING VOICE: "You have won 96 percent of your days
   over the last month. One Missed day in four weeks —
   and even that one you recovered from in 48 hours."

E3. GROWTH AREA TONE
Growth Areas should be developmental observations, not
criticism and not action items. Audit whether the tone
reads as:
- Coach sharing a perspective the athlete might not see
  (CORRECT)
- Coach telling the athlete what to improve (INCORRECT
  — that belongs in the CM action section)
- Coach being critical of the athlete's performance
  (INCORRECT — never)

------------------------------------------------------------
CATEGORY F: FORMAT AND PRESENTATION
------------------------------------------------------------

F1. SEPARATOR BAR CONSISTENCY
All CMs should use identical separator bars (full-width
━━━ characters). Check for inconsistencies across athletes
and across weeks.

F2. SIGNATURE CONSISTENCY
"— Coach Arron" everywhere. Check for variants ("— Arron,"
"— Coach A," missing signatures, etc.).

F3. YOUR ACTION SUBTITLE VARIATION
Each CM's YOUR ACTION section should have a contextual
subtitle (e.g., "BEFORE EACH DAY OF MT UNION," "FOR THIS
WEEK," "BEFORE PRACTICE MONDAY"). Check that these vary
and connect to the specific week's content.

F4. HEADER FORMAT CONSISTENCY
"VIRTUSFOCUS — ATHLETE COACHING MESSAGE" followed by
"[Name] | Week [N] | [Date Range]". Check for
inconsistencies.

F5. DEEP DIVE HEADER FORMAT
"DEEP DIVE COACHING ANALYSIS" followed by name/week/date.
Section headers in ALL CAPS with separator lines. Check
for format drift across files.

   - Does each athlete receive a distinct coaching voice?
   - Where does individuation succeed?

   - Week 1 vs. Week 7+ comparison results

5. WEEK-TYPE VOICE VARIATION ASSESSMENT
   - Gold Streak weeks vs. disrupted weeks
   - Competition weeks vs. recovery weeks
   - High EI weeks vs. Low EI weeks

6. SPECIFICATION IMPROVEMENT RECOMMENDATIONS
   Organized by priority (High / Medium / Low):
   - What should change in the Coaching Message Framework?
   - What should change in the Deep Dive Framework?
   - What should change in the brand voice or style guide?
   - What should change in the Coaching Output Instructions?
   - Any new rules or guidelines needed?

7. PHRASE FREQUENCY TABLE
   A table of the most frequently used phrases across all
   audited files, with counts and severity assessment.

8. EXEMPLAR PASSAGES

   most authentically sound like a real coach. Quote them
   in full with file citations. Explain what makes them
   work.

9. PROBLEM PASSAGES

10. CRITICAL OPERATING RULES
============================================================

- Do NOT modify any files. This is a read-only audit.
- Do NOT regenerate any output. This session produces a
  report, not corrected files.

  every cited example.

  goal is accurate assessment, not reassurance.
- Remember: these outputs go directly to real athletes.
  The stakes are real. An athlete who receives AI-sounding
  coaching messages will lose trust in the program.
