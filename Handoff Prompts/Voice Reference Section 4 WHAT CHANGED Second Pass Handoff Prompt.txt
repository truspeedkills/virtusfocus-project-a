3. CONTEXT - WHY THIS IS A SECOND PASS
============================================================

In Session 100, a first draft of all 8 Section 4 WHAT CHANGED
rewrites was produced and presented for user review. The user
asked for honest self-assessment. Five specific problems were
identified:

PROBLEM 1: Uneven depth across the 8 blocks
  Pairs 1, 4, 5, 6, 7 were strong. Pairs 2, 3, 8 were adequate
  but thinner. The thin blocks had either less analytical
  depth or stretched a single idea across two paragraphs
  when one would have sufficed.

PROBLEM 2: "A real coach does/does not..." construction crutch
  The construction "A real coach does/does not..." appeared
  5 times across 8 blocks (Pairs 1, 2, 3, 7, 8). This is the
  Section 4 equivalent of the "ranks" and "names" crutches
  that forced a second pass on Section 3. The construction
  works, but at 5 uses it becomes the document's signature
  move, which teaches the model to copy the pattern.

PROBLEM 3: Voice Principles named in only 4 of 8 blocks
  Pairs 1, 2, 3, 8 explicitly named principles (Start with
  Meaning Not Description, Spend Your Words Unevenly, Earn
  Every Sentence, Earn Every Sentence applied to transitions).
  Pairs 4, 5, 6, 7 did not. Section 3's second pass hit 14 of
  18 blocks; Section 4's first draft hit 4 of 8.

PROBLEM 4: Filler sentences in Pairs 3 and 8
  Pair 3 had "Directness that has to introduce itself is not
  directness. The announcement is the tell." Two sentences
  saying the same thing from two angles. Tighter: keep one.
  Pair 8 had a psychological explanation clause ("because
  announcing a shift is easier than making it") that did not
  teach what Coach Arron does differently - it explained the
  AI pattern's source rather than the fix.

PROBLEM 5: No consistent structural template across the 8 blocks
  The blocks opened in three different ways (abstract
  principle, declarative observation, quote). The analysis
  unfolded in different structures. Variety is good, but
  within the variety there was inconsistency in how each
  block reached its conclusion. A disciplined second pass
  should establish a loose shared structure (what the AI
  pattern does wrong at an instinct level, what the AFTER
  does to fix it, connection to a Voice Principle) while
  still varying the prose.

User approved the second pass but the session had accumulated
significant context from the Section 3 review and rewrite work.
Section 4 second pass was deferred to a fresh session to
maintain the same quality bar as Section 3 without context
pressure. That fresh session is this one.

DECISION 1: Second pass is needed
  The first draft was adequate but below the Section 3 quality
  bar. The user affirmed quality over speed ("saving time at
  the expense of quality is a non starter"). Do not attempt
  to shortcut this by applying the first draft with minor
  edits. Write the second pass from scratch against the five

DECISION 2: Five quality constraints (inherited from Section 3
            second pass)

  Constraint A - Vocabulary tracking
  Track pedagogical verb usage as you write. Cap any single
  verb at 2 uses maximum across the 8 blocks. Specifically
  watch for: "names," "ranks," "grounds," "delivers," "lands,"
  "arrives," "carries," "tells," "says," "offers," "holds,"
  "identifies," "points to," "draws from." These were the
  ceiling verbs in Section 3. If you use one, count it against
  the cap.

  Constraint B - Instinct-first framing
  Every block's opening sentence names the coaching reason
  before describing the rhetorical structure. Lead with WHY
  a coach would make this move, not WHAT the move is. The
  Section 3 WHY THIS WORKS rewrites all pass this test; read
  them for pattern reference.

  Constraint C - Variable length based on pair complexity
  Let each block find its own length between approximately 5
  and 10 sentences. Simple single-instinct pairs should be
  shorter. Multi-move pairs should be longer. Uniform length
  across all 8 blocks is a failure mode. Section 3's second
  pass ranged from 5 sentences (A2, A3, C1, C2) to 9 sentences
  (F1).

  Constraint D - Explicit Voice Principle references
  Name Voice Principles by name in 7 or 8 of 8 blocks. The
  six principles are:
    0. Service First
    1. Start with Meaning, Not Description
    2. Spend Your Words Unevenly
    3. Talk Like You've Been in the Room
    4. Earn Every Sentence
    5. React Don't Compose (Coach Arron Reacts, He Doesn't Compose)
  Section 3's second pass hit 14 of 18 blocks. Target similar
  density or higher for Section 4.

  Constraint E - Zero review-process jargon
  Do not use "load-bearing," "contrast-reframe," "softened
  contrast," "subordinated trailing clause," "pattern-adjacent,"
  or other analysis vocabulary that emerged during the review
  process. Write for a reader of the Voice Reference, not for
  someone reviewing the Voice Reference's design history.

DECISION 3: "A real coach" construction cap
  Cap the "A real coach does/does not" construction at 2 uses
  maximum across the 8 blocks. The first draft used it 5 times.
  This construction is rhetorically effective when deployed
  once or twice, and becomes a signature crutch at higher
  frequency. Use different contrasts for the other 6 blocks:
    - "A coach who has been watching for [N] weeks..."
    - "A coach sitting across from an athlete in Week N..."
    - "The athlete already [did/lived/wrote] X. The coach's job
      is Y."
    - "[AI pattern] works until the athlete notices the pattern."
    - Direct observation of what the BEFORE teaches the model
      to copy.
    - Negative framing of the AI pattern's effect on the athlete.
  Vary the framing. Do not default to the same contrast move.

DECISION 4: Zero em dashes (applies to all backend/pedagogical
            documents)
  The em dash ban established in Section 35 of the Coaching
  Output Instructions v2.2 applies to the Voice Reference as
  well, per user directive during Session 100. The current
  Voice Reference has 0 em dashes, verified. Do not introduce
  any. Use commas, periods, colons, or parentheses for
  parenthetical asides, mid-sentence pivots, list introductions,
  and dramatic pauses. See Section 35 C1 for replacement rules
  by function.

DECISION 5: Do not modify the BEFORE or AFTER text
  Only WHAT CHANGED prose blocks get rewritten. The BEFORE
  quotes are historical production output and should not be
  edited. The AFTER text was finalized during Session 100 and
  should not be changed. If you notice a problem with an AFTER
  during this session, flag it to the user but do not edit it
  without approval.

DECISION 6: End-of-document format pass is part of this session
  After the 8 WHAT CHANGED rewrites are complete and applied,
  run a search-and-replace pass converting any curly smart
  quotes to straight quotes throughout the Voice Reference.
  Curly quotes to convert:
    " (U+201C, left double quote)
    " (U+201D, right double quote)
    ' (U+2018, left single quote)
    ' (U+2019, right single quote)
  Replace with:
    " (U+0022, straight double quote)
    ' (U+0027, straight single quote, or apostrophe)

AI pattern the AFTER fixes: Stat-led template opening where

the same [streak count] + [week count] + [transition]

------------------------------------------------------------
PAIR 2: Recounting vs Interpreting
------------------------------------------------------------

BEFORE (typical pattern):
"Monday you went to the gym. Tuesday you had a good practice.
Wednesday you wrote in your journal about staying focused.
Thursday you had a difficult conversation with your coach.
Friday you took the day off. Saturday you hit the field with
your teammate."

AFTER:
"Thursday's conversation with your coach is what the week
turned on. You'd been moving toward it all week, and the way
you showed up after that moment told me it hit the way it
needed to."

AI pattern the AFTER fixes: Day-by-day recounting. Listing
what the athlete did is reading, not interpreting. The AFTER
replaces the list with the one moment that carries the week's
meaning.

------------------------------------------------------------
PAIR 3: Telegraphing
------------------------------------------------------------

BEFORE (Tucker Wk6 CM):
"Now I'm going to be direct about something. The
zero-Missed-day week happened because Easter gave you a
full schedule of activities with people you care about."

AFTER (Tucker Wk7 CM approach):
"The gym hasn't been part of the last few weeks. You've
shown you'll do the work at home. Now bring that same
standard into the gym."

AI pattern the AFTER fixes: Telegraphing - announcing a
coaching move before making it. "I'm going to be direct
about something" is not directness; it is preamble.

------------------------------------------------------------
PAIR 4: Trendline arc recitation
------------------------------------------------------------

BEFORE (Grace Wk8 DD):
"The eight-week trendline now carries two complete arcs.
The first is the confidence arc: outcome-reactive at
baseline, preparation-driven through Weeks 1-2,
structure-sustained under pressure in Week 3, action-

competition-validated in Week 6, and failure-resilient

AFTER (Grace Wk9 DD approach):

and creating your own answer with Kabine. This week was
you going straight to the person who makes the decisions.
You're not stuck reacting anymore. You're doing something
with it."

AI pattern the AFTER fixes: Trendline arc recitation with
hyphenated compound-adjective arc labels. The AFTER uses
three concrete moments instead of seven labeled phases.

------------------------------------------------------------
PAIR 5: Pipeline jargon
------------------------------------------------------------

BEFORE (Grace Wk8 DD):
"Consistent phase maintained through your eighth
consecutive Growth week. Emotional intensity returned to

AFTER:

AI pattern the AFTER fixes: Internal pipeline classification
vocabulary surfacing in athlete-facing output. The AFTER
translates category labels into the athlete's own experience.

------------------------------------------------------------
PAIR 6: Triple-negative AI pattern
------------------------------------------------------------

BEFORE (Grace Wk10 DD):
"This week brought something different. Not a failure,

AFTER:
"This was a quieter week. You didn't get any playing

AI pattern the AFTER fixes: Triple-negative setup "Not X,
not Y, not Z. Just W." The AFTER leads with positive
framing and supports it with specific context.

------------------------------------------------------------
PAIR 7: "The version of [X]" construction
------------------------------------------------------------

BEFORE (Grace Wk9 DD):
"The version of confidence you're carrying into PSACs

AFTER:
"The confidence you're taking into PSACs now isn't tied

weeks of showing up the same way whether the day gives
back or not."

AI pattern the AFTER fixes: "The version of [X]"
construction that turns an abstract quality into a
labeled object, creating distance between the athlete
and the quality being described.

------------------------------------------------------------

BEFORE (Grace Wk8 CM):

AFTER:

Instead of:
"Here is what earned recognition this week."

Instead of:
"Here is what needs attention."

AI pattern the AFTER fixes: "Here is what" transition
sentences that announce a shift without doing any coaching
work. The AFTER demonstrates the fix through two paired

12. EXAMPLE OF THE TARGET VOICE (FROM SECTION 3)
============================================================

This is the A1 WHY THIS WORKS block that was applied during
Session 100. It is 6 sentences, leads with the coaching
instinct, names a Voice Principle by name, has zero em dashes,
and contains no review-process jargon. Use it as the tonal
benchmark for Section 4 rewrites.

    WHY THIS WORKS:
    When the week was hard, a coach who moves straight to
    interpretation has already missed the first job. The
    opening marks Friday as the week's most important moment
    in a single sentence and then stays inside what Grace
    experienced (cracked, wanted to come home) before
    reaching for anything else. Only after the difficulty
    has been fully honored does the second paragraph turn to
    what Grace did: kept the routine, not because anyone
    told her to, but because it was hers. The quote pull of
    Grace's own line ("Honestly just doing it for me at this
    point") becomes the interpretive gift. The coach tells
    Grace what her own sentence means, which she could not
    do for herself. This is Service First applied to an
    empathy-first week: the coach asks what Grace needs this
    week and decides it is someone to stay in the hard place
    with her before offering anywhere else to go.

Notes on why this works as a model for Section 4:
  - Opens with "When the week was hard, a coach who..." This
    is instinct-first framing.
  - Uses parentheses for parenthetical aside (cracked, wanted
    to come home) instead of em dashes.
  - Names Service First explicitly in the closing sentence.
  - Paragraph structure follows the exemplar's structure
    (opening empathy, second paragraph turn, quote pull,
    closing principle).
  - Concrete and specific throughout ("Friday," "the routine,"
    "Honestly just doing it for me at this point").
  - No review-process jargon. No "load-bearing." No "subordinated
    trailing clause." The prose is about the coaching, not the
    analysis.

Section 4 rewrites should sound like they came from the same
writer as this block, adapted to the Before/After Pair format
instead of the exemplar format. The voice rhymes, the
structure adapts.
