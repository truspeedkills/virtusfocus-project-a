YOUR ROLE AND YOUR DAY
====================================================

WHY THIS ROLE MATTERS

Note-takers are the most important under-celebrated role
in the room. The audio recording will catch every word.
The worksheets will capture each attendee's private
written answers. But neither captures the read of the
room. Who deferred to whom. Who visibly disagreed but did
not say anything. When the room agreed without saying so.
When somebody pushed back politely on a question that
should be reopened. That is what you capture, and there
is no other way to get it.

You signed an NDA. You are a trusted observer. You are
not asked to be a researcher or a transcriptionist. You
are asked to read the room with attention and write down
what you notice in a simple shorthand we will teach you.

YOUR JOB IN ONE PARAGRAPH

You sit slightly off to the side of one of three breakout
groups during the 1:30-2:15 PM block. You do not speak.
You write down what people say (or paraphrase it), tag
each entry with a sentiment code, and capture the body-
language signals the audio cannot pick up. You collect
the worksheets at the end of the breakout. You then join
the rest of the room as a participant for the remaining
45 minutes (2:15-3:00 PM). After the event, you hand
your notes to Arron or Nicole.

====================================================
THE SHORTHAND CONVENTION
====================================================

Every entry in your notebook follows this format:

  [TIME]  [TIER]  [INITIALS]  [CODE]  [content]

Aim for a timestamp at the start of every meaningful
entry. Helps synthesis align your notes with the audio
transcript.

SENTIMENT CODES

Single letter, the most important part of every entry.

  P  = Positive / agreement / engaged
  N  = Negative / disagreement / pushback
  M  = Mixed / "yes but" / middle
  Q  = Question raised by attendee, worth following up
  *  = Cross-lens insight (attendee bringing their
       secondary perspective into the discussion)

TIER PREFIX  (INSTITUTIONAL ONLY)

D2C and GTM note-takers leave this blank.

  HS   = High school coach is answering (Rick, Gio, Buck)
  COL  = College coach is answering (Bret, Bob, Chris)

VERBATIM RULES

  Verbatim quote in double quotes for exact words:
    "We would never deploy this without legal review."

  Paraphrase in parens when you cannot capture verbatim:
    (paraphrased: legal review is the gate)

  Body language and structural notes go in plain text:
    Rick visibly disagreed but did not say anything.
    Room went quiet for several seconds after this.

  Mark uncertain hearing with [unclear]:
    "We would [unclear] without legal."

====================================================
DURING AND AFTER THE BREAKOUT
====================================================

WHAT TO CAPTURE

  Verbatim quotes when something quotable lands. Examples
    of quotable: a sharp turn of phrase, a pointed
    criticism, a vivid analogy, a reframe of the question.

  Sentiment shifts. When the energy in the group pivots,
    write it down: "Room agreed without saying so." Or:
    "Quiet pause after Tom's pushback."

  Cross-lens moments (asterisk code). When somebody
    brings their secondary perspective. These are the
    highest-value entries because the audio plus
    transcript may not flag them automatically.

  Disagreements that go unsaid. When one attendee
    visibly reacts to another but does not speak. Audio
    cannot catch that. You can.

  Questions the room raised that deserve follow-up.
    Q-coded entries become the open-questions section
    of the synthesis report.

WHAT NOT TO CAPTURE (AND WHY)

  Do NOT try to transcribe word-for-word. The audio
    recording handles that. You handle SENTIMENT and
    STRUCTURE.

  Do NOT summarize in real time. Capture moments, not
    summaries. Synthesis builds the summary from your
    moments plus the transcript.

  Do NOT editorialize in your notes. "I think Tom is
    right" has no place in your notebook. Your job is to
    capture what they said and how they said it, not
    what you thought of it.

  Do NOT participate in the discussion. If your
    facilitator asks you something, you can answer
    briefly, then go back to taking notes. Otherwise
    you stay quiet.

  Do NOT make eye contact with attendees in a way that
    invites being addressed. Sit slightly off to the
    side, neutral expression.

  Do NOT use real names of athletes if they come up in
    discussion. Athletes are anonymized as Athlete A,
    Athlete B, Athlete C in all materials. If an
    attendee guesses or names a real athlete, capture
    that they did so but do not reproduce the name.
