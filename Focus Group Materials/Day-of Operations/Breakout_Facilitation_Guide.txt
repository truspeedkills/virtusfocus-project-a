====================================================
WHAT A BREAKOUT IS
====================================================

A breakout is when the full room splits into smaller
discussion groups (3-6 people each) running simultaneously
in parallel for a fixed time window. Three breakouts at
this event run at the same time during the 1:30-2:15
block:

  INSTITUTIONAL    6 attendees   Arron facilitates
  D2C / ATHLETE    4 attendees   Nicole facilitates
  GTM              4 attendees   Michele Cribbs facilitates

Each breakout group sits at its own table or section of
the room, has its own facilitator, has its own note-taker,
and discusses its own segment-specific worksheet questions
for 45 minutes.

Three parallel discussions, three separate signal streams,
all captured for synthesis later.


====================================================
WHY BREAKOUTS WORK BETTER THAN ONE BIG-ROOM DISCUSSION
====================================================

If we did all 14 attendees in one room for 45 minutes,
two things would happen.

First, the loudest 3 to 4 voices would dominate.
Quieter participants would defer to whoever spoke first.

Second, the questions would be too generic to extract
segment-specific signal. Institutional coaches and
parents press hard on different things. Splitting into
segments lets each room go deep on what matters to it.

The synthesis benefit: at the end, we get three distinct
segment reads we can compare side-by-side, instead of one
homogenized full-room discussion.


====================================================
PHYSICAL SETUP
====================================================

Hampton Inn room is reserved all day. The three breakouts
likely share the same physical room with chairs grouped
into three clusters. Audio bleed between clusters is
acceptable for this purpose because each breakout records
audio separately. If a sub-group needs more separation,
the venue probably has secondary space available.

Each breakout cluster should have:

  Round-table or tight-circle seating for 4 to 6 people
  One designated facilitator chair (slightly more visible)
  One designated note-taker chair (corner, less visible)
  Voice recorder placed centrally on the table
  Worksheets in front of each attendee
  Pen or pencil for each attendee


====================================================
THE FACILITATOR'S ROLE
====================================================

Three things the facilitator does, in order of importance.

1. ASKS THE WORKSHEET QUESTIONS IN SEQUENCE AND MANAGES
   DISCUSSION TIME

Each segment has 5 questions plus a free-form page,
which works out to roughly 7 to 8 minutes per question
across 45 minutes (with a small buffer). Facilitator
opens with Q1, hears responses, moves to Q2 when
discussion is wrapping or when about 8 minutes have
passed. They are the time-keeper for the room.

2. DRAWS OUT QUIETER VOICES AND PREVENTS ONE PERSON
   DOMINATING

If three of four attendees have weighed in and one has
not, facilitator says: "Tom, you have been quiet on
this. What is your read?"

If one person is monologuing, facilitator says: "Hold
that thought for a second. Liz, you reacted to that.
What is your push-back?"

3. PROBES CROSS-LENS SECONDARY PERSPECTIVES AT THE
   RIGHT MOMENT

The cross-lens flags in the pickup file (Buck's
father-son dynamic, Chris's FBS-vs-D2 contrast, Liz's
youth-coaching lens, etc.) are deliberate probe-points.
Facilitator listens for the natural moment to ask:
"Buck, given that you work under your son as head coach,
how does the parent-facing layer read to you specifically?"


WHAT THE FACILITATOR DOES NOT DO

  Take notes (the note-taker does that)
  Defend the product (this is a focus group, let
    attendees criticize)
  Answer technical questions in detail (defer to
    "great question, hold for the synthesis room")
  Spend more than 30 seconds explaining anything
    (every minute facilitator talks is a minute
    attendees are not talking)


====================================================
THE NOTE-TAKER'S ROLE
====================================================

Note-taker observes, captures, and stays out of the
conversation.

WHAT THEY CAPTURE

  Verbatim quotes when something quotable lands.
    Example: "Position coaches will not read it. They
    will skim the color and move on."

  Sentiment codes (P / N / M / Q / *) defined in
    Capture_Protocol.txt SENTIMENT CODES section.

  For the Institutional breakout only: HS or COL
    tier prefix on every entry, defined in the
    Capture_Protocol.txt TIER PREFIX section.

  Body-language signals the audio will not catch.
    Example: "Rick visibly disagreed but did not say
    anything."

  Cross-lens moments when an attendee brings their
    secondary perspective into a primary-segment
    question. Mark with the asterisk sentiment code.

  Time stamps every 5 minutes for synchronization.

WHAT THE NOTE-TAKER DOES NOT DO

  Participate in discussion
  Defend or explain the product
  Try to capture every word (the audio recording
    handles verbatim, the note-taker handles SENTIMENT
    and STRUCTURE)
  Make eye contact in a way that invites being
    addressed (sit slightly off to the side)

The reason note-takers are non-participants: their
job is to read the room interpretively, not to add
another voice. If your note-taker also wants to be in
the discussion, they are a participant in the wrong
seat.


====================================================
TIME MECHANICS ACROSS THE 45-MINUTE WINDOW
====================================================

  0:00 to 0:02
    Facilitator does a 30-second intro:
    "We are the [Institutional / D2C / GTM] breakout.
    Here is how it works: I will ask each question, you
    discuss, we move on when it is wrapping. The
    worksheets in front of you are for your own private
    answers. Write whatever you want, do not worry about
    agreeing with the group. We collect them at the end.
    Everything you say is on the audio, covered by your
    NDA. Let's go."

  0:02 to 0:42
    Five questions, roughly 7 to 8 minutes each.
    Facilitator manages time. Note-taker captures.

  0:42 to 0:45
    Wrap. Facilitator says: "Couple minutes. Finish your
    worksheets, take any free-form notes you did not get
    to."
    Note-taker collects worksheets as people stand.

  0:45
    Room reconvenes for the pricing and deployment block
    at 2:15 to 2:45.


====================================================
THE INSTITUTIONAL SOFT-SPLIT (PATH 2)
====================================================

This applies ONLY to Arron's 6-person Institutional
breakout. D2C and GTM run standard structure (above).

For each of the 5 questions, Arron does this:

  1. Reads the question.
  2. Says: "Let's start with the HS lens. Rick, want to
     lead off?"
  3. Hears 3 responses (Rick, Gio, Buck) in roughly 3 to
     4 minutes.
  4. Says: "Now college lens. Same question, possibly
     different answer. Bret?"
  5. Hears 3 responses (Bret, Bob, Chris) in roughly 3
     to 4 minutes.
  6. If time allows: "Quick cross-tier. Does the HS-vs-
     college divide here change anything you would add?"
     Roughly 1 to 2 minutes.
  7. Moves to the next question.

The note-taker tags every entry with HS or COL prefix.
Synthesis later separates the tier reads cleanly.


====================================================
WHAT THE ATTENDEES DO
====================================================

Each attendee gets a 7-page worksheet (cover plus 5
question pages plus 1 free-form). They:

  Write their own answers privately on the worksheet
    while or after discussion. Not required to match
    group consensus.
  Speak when called on or when they have something
    to add.
  Can leave the NAME field blank if they want
    anonymity (segment is what matters).
  Hand the worksheet to the note-taker at end of
    breakout.


====================================================
WHAT HAPPENS AFTER BREAKOUTS (2:15 ONWARD)
====================================================

  2:15 to 2:45  PRICING AND DEPLOYMENT BLOCK
    Full room reconvenes. Arron and Nicole co-lead a
    free-form discussion on pricing direction and
    institutional GTM strategy. This is where attendees
    press hardest. Note-takers from breakouts join the
    discussion now (they do not note-take this block,
    they participate).

  2:45 to 3:00  CROSS-ROOM SYNTHESIS
    Full room. Arron asks each breakout to share one or
    two top findings: "Rick, what was your loudest take
    from your room?"
    Attendees hear what came out of OTHER groups and
    react in real time. Powerful end note.

====================================================
CONTINGENCIES
====================================================

ATTENDEE GOES SILENT FOR ENTIRE BREAKOUT
  Facilitator addresses directly twice. If still silent,
  let it go. Note-taker captures the silence as an
  observation.

DISCUSSION RUNS HOT (PEOPLE TALKING OVER EACH OTHER)
  Facilitator says: "Hold up, one at a time. [Name],
  finish your point. [Other name], you are next."

ATTENDEE WANTS TO TALK ABOUT SOMETHING NOT IN THE
WORKSHEET
  Facilitator says: "Hold that for the free-form page,
  or the synthesis block at the end. Right now we are
  on Q2." This protects the time budget.

ATTENDEE ASKS A POINTED PRODUCT QUESTION (e.g., "What
exactly does your audit layer detect?")
  Facilitator says: "Great question, hold it for the
  pricing and deployment block at 2:15. We will get to
  it then." This avoids facilitator defending the
  product mid-breakout.

VOICE RECORDER FAILS
  Note-taker switches to detailed verbatim mode. Backup
  phone recorder activates if available.
  Synthesis weighs that breakout's findings slightly
  lower in cross-segment confidence.

SOMEONE WANTS TO LEAVE EARLY
  Let them. Note in the roster. Synthesis will reflect
  partial participation.
