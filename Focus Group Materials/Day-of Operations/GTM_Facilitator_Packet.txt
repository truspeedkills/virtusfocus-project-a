WHAT GTM PROBES

The GTM worksheet asks five questions designed to stress-test the go-to-market story. Your attendees are not here to learn about VirtusFocus. They are here to find what is wrong with the positioning, the category, the channel choice, and the pricing.

  Q1  Pitch stress-test. Where the story lands and stumbles.
  Q2  Category and competition. Who do we compete with.
  Q3  Single app vs. white-label architecture.
  Q4  React to the artifact. What sells, what proof is missing.
  Q5  Killer test. Path, first $1M, what NOT to do.


WHAT SIGNAL MATTERS MOST

Three things you are most listening for, in priority order.

1. SURPRISE. The thing they noticed that the pitch did not address. The objection we did not anticipate.

2. DIVERGENCE. When two attendees disagree on something fundamental (single app vs. white-label, D2C vs. institutional first). Capture both sides verbatim.

3. SPECIFICITY. "I would not market it like that" is good. "I would market it like THIS instead" is better. Push for the second when you can without being leading.

YOUR FIVE QUESTIONS
====================================================

Read each question to the room verbatim. Give 60 to 90 seconds of quiet for written answers. Then open discussion. Watch your time. Roughly 8 to 9 minutes per question including write time and discussion. The breakout is 45 minutes total.


QUESTION 1: PITCH STRESS-TEST (~8 MIN)

Verbatim prompt: "Forget what we've shown you. In one sentence, how would you describe VirtusFocus to someone who's never heard of it? Where does the story land? Where does it stumble?"

What it probes: The pitch's actual reading at first contact. We will hear our own language echoed back, and we will hear where it does not translate.

Follow-ups (use only if discussion stalls):

- "When you say it 'lands', land for whom?"
- "When it 'stumbles', what part are you mentally editing as you describe it?"
- "What word are you replacing in your sentence that we used in ours?"

Trap to avoid: Defending the pitch. If someone says "the part about mental performance vs. mental health is confusing," do not explain it. Note the signal. Move on.

Watch for: JR likely answers in B2B language. TP likely answers in consumer-shelf language. NC likely answers in parent-emotional language. JA likely answers in competitive-category language. The four sentences they produce are four different positioning targets. All four are signal.


QUESTION 2: CATEGORY AND COMPETITION (~9 MIN)

Verbatim prompt: "What category does this compete in, in your head? Who are we up against? How do we differentiate fast, in one line a parent or coach would actually repeat?"

What it probes: Where attendees mentally file VirtusFocus. The category we think we are in may not be the category they put us in. Differentiation language testing.

Follow-ups:

- "Why that category and not the other one named above?"
- "Who is the most direct competitor, by name?"
- "If you had to repeat that one-line differentiator to your spouse tonight, would you remember it tomorrow?"

Trap to avoid: Naming competitors yourself. Let the attendees name them. If no one says a competitor name in the first three minutes, ask "who specifically?" but do not seed.

Watch for: JA's answer here is the highest-priority capture of the entire breakout. She knows the competitive landscape because she sells in an adjacent space. If TP says "this competes with energy drinks for athletic mindshare", that is a category insight we have not considered. Capture verbatim.


QUESTION 3: SINGLE APP vs WHITE-LABEL (~10 MIN)

Verbatim prompt: "We're betting both channels are viable. Institutional AND D2C. Two architectures: the same app serves both, or we build a white-label version institutions can skin with their own brand. Ohio State gets 'Ohio State Mental Performance' with their colors. Which is right? Why? What does each cost us that we're not seeing?"

What it probes: This is the most strategically loaded question in the worksheet. The decision here determines product roadmap, engineering investment, and pricing structure for the next two years. The answer we get here directly informs the May 30 go/no-go gate.

Follow-ups:

- "If both, who pays for the second engineering track?"
- "What does Ohio State actually want with their skin? Logo? Colors? Their own coaching content? Their own roster?"
- "What is the hidden cost of the same-app approach when we land Ohio State?"

Trap to avoid: Letting the room drift to checkbox voting before discussion. Open the discussion before referencing the checkboxes on the worksheet. The reasoning matters more than the checkbox.

Watch for: JR will think about institutional-sales mechanics. His answer to "what does each cost us" is concrete. TP will think about brand dilution risk if we white-label. Capture the brand-dilution language verbatim. Divergence here between attendees is signal. Do not push for consensus. Capture the disagreement.


QUESTION 4: REACT TO THE ARTIFACT (~9 MIN)

Verbatim prompt: "You've reviewed Grace's coaching output and the dashboard mockups. If you were marketing this tomorrow, what's the headline asset? What story actually sells it? What proof are we missing?"

What it probes: Marketing-asset prioritization. Which artifact in the GTM packet is the strongest selling tool. What proof is missing from the current set.

Follow-ups:

- "Which artifact would you put on the homepage?"
- "What story do you tell the parent first, before showing any artifact?"
- "What proof point is missing: outcome data, endorsement, athlete testimonial?"

Trap to avoid: Defending why the current artifact set is what it is. If they say "I would never lead with the Coach Insight", do not explain why we lead with it. Capture the rejection.

Watch for: NC will likely respond to the Parent CM viscerally. If she says something like "this is what I wish I had when my kid was a freshman", capture verbatim. That is the D2C parent-channel north star line. TP will likely respond to whichever artifact has the strongest visual hook. He thinks in shelf appeal.


QUESTION 5: KILLER TEST (~8 MIN)

Verbatim prompt: "You're advising us on GTM strategy. Which path? D2C first, institutional first, independent paths, or simultaneously? What does the first $1M of revenue look like? What would you absolutely NOT do?"

What it probes: Path priority and explicit stop-doing items. The "what would you NOT do" prompt often produces the most directional signal because it reveals where attendees see real failure modes.

Follow-ups:

- "What does the first $1M look like in months, not dollars? Who buys, in what order?"
- "Of the things you would NOT do, which one are we most at risk of doing anyway?"
- "Would you change your answer if I told you we are bootstrapping vs. raising?" (only ask if discussion is light)

Trap to avoid: Letting the discussion turn into consensus-building. Capture each attendee's individual path choice (the checkbox AND the reasoning) before you let them argue with each other. Divergence between four senior operators is the most valuable signal of the day.

Watch for: All four "would NOT do" answers are gold. Capture each one verbatim. If two attendees independently name the same NOT-do item, that is a stop-doing decision the founders make at the May 30 gate.


====================================================
SEGMENT-SPECIFIC DYNAMICS
====================================================

YOUR ATTENDEES KNOW EACH OTHER

You recommended all four to this room. They likely know each other socially or professionally. Watch for two risks specific to a known-relationships breakout.

1. PILE-ON. One attendee makes a strong claim and the others nod or agree without independently answering. Counter by going around the table: "I want to hear each of you answer this one individually before we discuss."

2. COURTESY DEFLECTION. Attendees soften criticism because they know each other. Counter by saying "We are looking for what would NOT work. Be direct. We will not be in the room when you are direct, only the recording."


THE Q3 ARCHITECTURE QUESTION IS THE STRATEGIC PEAK

If you have to compress any question, do not compress Q3. The white-label vs. single-app decision is the single most expensive product-roadmap call we make in the next 90 days. If discussion is rich at Q3, give it 12 minutes and pull from Q4 or Q5.


THE PARENT CROSS-LENS WITH NOELE

NC wears two hats in this room. She is a GTM advisor AND a parent of collegiate athletes. Her reaction to the Parent CM and Parent Insight will be different from her reaction to the institutional pitch. Make space for both reads. If she says "as a parent" or "speaking as a mom", capture that as a tag in your notes. That content is D2C-grade signal coming from a GTM seat.


THE COMPETITIVE-CATEGORY ANSWER FROM JENNIFER

Jennifer Altimore's response to Q2 is the most strategic single piece of signal in your entire breakout. She operates in mental-performance-adjacent space professionally. When she names the competitor or category, it is informed by direct selling experience, not abstract market analysis. Slow the room down when she answers. Make sure the note-taker captures her exact words.


CLINICAL LANGUAGE FROM JENNIFER (COMPLIANCE)

Hypnotherapy is clinical-adjacent. If JA frames an answer in clinical language, like "we help athletes with anxiety" or "we treat performance issues", do not echo, endorse, or build on the framing. If she explicitly invokes hypnotherapy as a comparison ("VirtusFocus is like hypnotherapy in that..."), let it sit. Capture verbatim. Do not validate or refute. We will analyze in synthesis.
