YOUR FIVE QUESTIONS
====================================================

Read each question to the room verbatim. Give 60 to 90 seconds of quiet for written answers. Then open discussion. Roughly 8 to 9 minutes per question. The breakout is 45 minutes total.


QUESTION 1: MEMORY ANCHOR (~9 MIN)

Verbatim prompt: "Think back to your competitive years, collegiate or high school. What's the thing your coaches and your program never gave you on the mental side that you needed? Confidence stuff, pressure stuff, recovery from mistakes, identity stuff, the quiet stuff. What you needed. What got in the way of you getting it."

What it probes: The honest gap between what they needed as athletes and what their coaching environment delivered. The unsentimental retrospective is the most direct read on what the product should provide.

Follow-ups (use only if discussion stalls):

- "When you say you needed X, can you remember a specific moment where the gap mattered?"
- "What did you do instead, given the gap?"
- "What would have made the difference, if anything was offered?"

Trap to avoid: Letting attendees frame the gap as "well, that's just sports". Push past the resignation. The gap is the product.

Watch for: Tamara's Seton Hill softball answer is the closest first-person alignment to Athlete A. Ron's Seton Hill wrestling answer plus his current Swampfox youth-program work creates a then-vs-now read. Liz's youth-coaching observation is not her own memory but observation of the same gap in current youth athletes.


QUESTION 2: ADOPTION-BARRIER EXTRACTION (~9 MIN)

Verbatim prompt: "If a tool like this had existed during your career, daily mindset prompts, weekly coaching created from your own data, parent or coach integration where your data and input stays private, would you have actually used it? Honestly. Yes, no, or depends. Why? What would have made it sticky? What would have made it just another thing on your phone you ignore?"

What it probes: Whether they would actually have used this. The "stays private" privacy guarantee is in the prompt because we suspected privacy would be the adoption barrier; the answers tell us whether that guarantee actually moves the needle.

Follow-ups:

- "When you say 'depends', depends on what specifically?"
- "What is the moment in your day this would have to fit into for you to use it?"
- "What is the one thing your other apps do that we would have to do too?"

Trap to avoid: Letting "stays private" be assumed. Probe it. Ask "did the privacy line in the question matter to your answer? If so, how?"

Watch for: Alex's answer reflects his beta-tester lens. Take it at face value. Ron's answer pulls from athlete-self plus current athletes he sees. The age-of-athlete dimension shows up here: what works for a 14-year-old wrestler does not work for a 21-year-old softball player.


QUESTION 3: STRESS-TEST OUR ASSUMPTION (~8 MIN)

Verbatim prompt: "Our working assumption: today's athletes are either getting nothing structured around mental performance, or they're getting talks and meetings that don't stick. True, untrue, or partially true? From your kids, your athletes, your network, what have you actually seen?"

What it probes: Whether our central market read is correct. The follow-up is where the real depth lives. We get four direct-observation accounts from four different athletic ecosystems.

Follow-ups:

- "Whose program is doing it best, by name?"
- "What specifically did they do that worked, even partially?"
- "What's the most common thing programs do that doesn't work?"

Trap to avoid: Letting the checkbox answer carry the weight. The checkbox is weak signal. The "what have you actually seen" prose is where the value is.

Watch for: Liz's youth-program direct observation is the most current. Ron's Swampfox observation plus his Seton Hill comparison is the most longitudinal. Tamara's network observation may pull from the legal community, which is a different kind of signal.


QUESTION 4: REACT TO GRACE'S ARC (~9 MIN)

Verbatim prompt: "You've reviewed Grace's coaching arc, the messages and deep dives she received over twelve weeks. If this were your daughter, your athlete, your younger self, would you want this? What's the strongest part? What's the part that would feel less authentic?"

What it probes: First-person and proxy reactions to the actual coaching output. The "less authentic" framing is intentional: we did not ask "what feels off or invasive". We asked "what feels less authentic". That is a higher bar that probes AI-coaching authenticity, not creepiness.

Follow-ups:

- "When you read the Wk7 failure-recovery message, what landed and what didn't?"
- "If your kid showed you this, what's the first question you would ask them?"
- "Where does it sound like Coach Arron, and where does it sound like a system pretending?"

Trap to avoid: Defending the coaching voice. If someone says a passage feels less authentic, do not explain why it was written that way. Capture the exact passage they flagged.

Watch for: Tamara's Seton-Hill-softball alignment makes her "if this were my younger self" answer the highest-fidelity capture. Liz's youth-coaching lens may surface a "less authentic" read specific to younger athletes that older readers would miss. Alex's beta-tester insider depth means he will critique craft, not concept.


QUESTION 5: KILLER TEST (~8 MIN)

Verbatim prompt: "If you paid for this directly, what's the monthly price ceiling? Where does it stop being worth it? Dollars per month. Why. What's the one thing that would make you cancel inside of a month?"

What it probes: Direct pricing read and the most acute cancel trigger. The dollar figure is the headline number, but the "why" and the cancel trigger are where the value is.

Follow-ups:

- "If we charged $5 less, would you commit longer?"
- "If you cancelled, would you tell other parents and athletes why? What would you say?"
- "What's the threshold where you would pay double if it delivered double?"

Trap to avoid: Letting the room converge on a single number. Each attendee should write their own price ceiling. Capture each verbatim. Convergence is signal, but divergence is more useful.

Watch for: Tamara's price-ceiling answer carries weight because she has professional decision authority. Liz's price-ceiling answer reflects youth-program parent psychology (what parents pay for kids' programs). Ron's reflects athlete-direct purchase or athlete-with-parent-paying dynamics. Alex's reflects insider economics.


====================================================
SEGMENT-SPECIFIC DYNAMICS
====================================================

THE PRIVACY GUARANTEE IN Q2 IS DELIBERATE

The Q2 prompt includes "your data and input stays private". This was added at Arron's direction because we suspected the privacy concern was the largest unspoken adoption barrier. If attendees do not engage with the privacy line in their answer, probe it directly: "Did the privacy guarantee matter to your answer?" The answer (yes, no, or "I didn't notice it") is signal.


THE LESS-AUTHENTIC FRAMING IN Q4

We deliberately did not ask "what would feel off or invasive". We asked "what would feel less authentic". This is a higher bar. "Off or invasive" probes creepiness; "less authentic" probes whether AI-coaching can feel like real coaching. Hold the framing if attendees drift to creepiness. Redirect: "Less authentic isn't quite the same as creepy. Where does it stop sounding like a coach who gets it?"


THE Q5 DOLLAR-AMOUNT FIELD

The worksheet has an explicit dollar field. Make sure each attendee writes a number. If anyone hesitates, say "even an estimate, just write what comes to mind first." The number is the headline; the discussion gives it context.
