WHAT INSTITUTIONAL PROBES

The Institutional worksheet asks five questions designed to surface what coaches actually do today, what they wish they could do, and what would have to be true for them to take this to their AD or principal for budget approval.

  Q1  Past state. What was your program doing about mental
      performance? What worked, what didn't.
  Q2  Failure-moment extraction. The last time something
      went wrong on the mental side.
  Q3  Stress-test our assumption that programs default to
      sporadic meetings, on-staff coaches, or guest speakers
      that don't translate.
  Q4  React to Athlete A's 12-week arc.
  Q5  Killer test. How would this make them win more, and
      what is the one piece that would have to be undeniable
      before they take it to their AD.


THE SOFT-SPLIT STRUCTURE (PATH 2)

This is the only breakout in the room running a structured tier order. Six attendees, three high school and three college. For each question:

1. HS coaches answer first. Rick, Gio, Buck. Each gets time on the question before the discussion broadens.

2. College coaches answer second. Bret, Bob, Chris. They reframe their answer in college context if the answer differs from the HS reads.

3. Open discussion. After both tiers have spoken individually, the discussion can flow freely.

The note-taker (Tucker) tags each entry HS or COL prefix in shorthand, so at synthesis time the HS-take and college-take are separable signal even though the discussion was joint.


WHAT SIGNAL MATTERS MOST

Three things you are most listening for, in priority order.

1. THE TIER DIVERGENCE. Where the HS read differs from the college read. The same question may produce two different answers, and both are signal. Capture both verbatim.

2. THE FAILURE MOMENT (Q2). Coaches do not talk about specific mental-performance failures with athletes very often. When they do, the language they use is the language a good coaching tool needs to speak. Capture verbatim.

3. THE AD-APPROVAL CONCRETE (Q5b). The "one piece that would have to be undeniable" is the marketing brief, the demo brief, and the proof brief. Get specific.

YOUR FIVE QUESTIONS
====================================================

Read each question to the room verbatim. Give 60 to 90 seconds of quiet for written answers. Then run the soft-split: HS first, then college. Then open discussion. Roughly 8 to 9 minutes per question. The breakout is 45 minutes total.


QUESTION 1: PAST STATE (~9 MIN)

Verbatim prompt: "When you think about your athletes' mental performance, confidence under pressure, recovery from setbacks, focus, identity through adversity, what was your program doing about it? What worked? What didn't?"

What it probes: The actual past-state of mental-performance work in their programs. Most coaches default to "we talk to them" or "we have a sports psychologist on call" or "we don't really do anything structured". Capture each variant.

Soft-split running order:

- Rick goes first. His answer covers the longest arc.
- Gio second. Mid-career HS context.
- Buck third. Assistant-coach view, possibly different from head-coach view.
- Bret fourth. College WR position context.
- Bob fifth. Program-builder context.
- Chris sixth. FBS-to-D2 contrast.

Follow-ups (use only if discussion stalls):

- "When you say 'we did X', who actually delivered it?"
- "What did the athletes do with what you delivered?"
- "What did you NOT do that you wish you had?"

Trap to avoid: Defending the gap. If a coach says "we did basically nothing", do not soften it. Do not say "well, you have practice talks". Capture the gap honestly. The honest gap is the product opportunity.

Watch for: Tier divergence between HS and college. HS coaches may report less formal structure (no sports psychologist budget). College coaches may report more formal structure that didn't translate. Both reads are signal.


QUESTION 2: FAILURE-MOMENT EXTRACTION (~9 MIN)

Verbatim prompt: "Walk us through the last time something went wrong with an athlete on the mental side. Pressure response, slump, locker-room thing, motivation collapse. The situation. What you actually did. What you wish you could have done."

What it probes: This is the highest-fidelity capture in the worksheet. Coaches can talk in abstractions about mental performance for hours, but a specific story exposes the exact moment where the current toolkit ran out.

Soft-split running order: Same as Q1.

Follow-ups:

- "When you say you 'tried X', what were the words you used to that athlete in that moment?"
- "What did the athlete actually need from you?"
- "What would you have given them if you had the right tool?"

Trap to avoid: Letting the discussion get philosophical. Pull people back to the specific moment. The story is the signal, not the lesson they extracted from the story.

Watch for: Rick's story is likely the most concrete. Bob's may be from an NFL context. Chris's may pull from Pitt under Narduzzi. The cross-tier pattern (HS coach failure-moment vs. college coach failure-moment) is signal for whether the same coaching gap exists at both levels.


QUESTION 3: STRESS-TEST OUR ASSUMPTION (~8 MIN)

Verbatim prompt: "Our working assumption: most programs spinning up mental performance solutions land on sporadic meetings, on-staff coaches, or guest speakers, and those resources don't translate into anything athletes use day-to-day. True, untrue, or partially true? Why? What programs you respect are actually doing that works?"

What it probes: Whether our pitch's central premise is correct. The checkbox forces a position. The follow-up "what programs you respect are doing" produces the comparative landscape that we cannot get from market research.

Soft-split running order: HS first, college second.

Follow-ups:

- "Which program did it best, by name?"
- "What specifically did they do that worked?"
- "What did they NOT do that we should know about?"

Trap to avoid: Letting attendees vote on the checkbox without justifying. The checkbox alone is weak signal. The "Why" and the "programs you respect" answers are where the value is.

Watch for: Chris will likely have the most specific FBS-program comparison data (from the Pitt years). Rick may name a specific HS program in Western PA. Bob may name an NFL or college program. Capture all program names verbatim.


QUESTION 4: REACT TO ATHLETE A (~9 MIN)

Verbatim prompt: "You've reviewed Athlete A's coaching insights, her Wk7 failure-recovery output, and her Wk12 peak output. What does this 12-week arc tell you that your coaching tools didn't? What would you want to know about her that this doesn't show you?"

What it probes: Whether the Coach Insights output is actually useful for institutional adoption. The "what would you want to know that this doesn't show you" answer surfaces the most valuable feature gap or addition.

Soft-split running order: HS first, college second.

Follow-ups:

- "What surprised you in the 12-week arc?"
- "What would you have done differently as her coach if you had this in Week 4?"
- "What is the one thing missing from this output that you would put first?"

Trap to avoid: Defending the format. If a coach says "the Coach Insight is too long" or "the format is too dense", do not explain why it is the way it is. Capture the rejection.

Watch for: Chris is the closest match to who would use the Coach Insights dashboard. His reaction to Wk12 versus Wk7 outputs is the highest-priority signal in this question. His feature-gap answer is the institutional product roadmap signal.


QUESTION 5: KILLER TEST (~8 MIN)

Verbatim prompt: "How would something like this make you win more, concretely? What is the ONE piece of this that would have to be undeniable before you would take it to your AD for budget approval?"

What it probes: The win-more concrete (which clarifies what coaches actually optimize for) and the AD-approval undeniable (which clarifies the budget-justification proof point we need to build).

Soft-split running order: HS first, college second.

Follow-ups:

- "When you say 'win more', do you mean more games, fewer slumps, better recruiting, or something else?"
- "If your AD said yes, who would lead the rollout to your athletes?"
- "What would you need to see in Year 1 for this to renew in Year 2?"

Trap to avoid: Letting the room agree on a single "undeniable proof". Capture each attendee's answer individually before discussion. Convergence on one proof point is signal, but divergence is more useful.

Watch for: Bob's answer to Q5b is the highest-priority capture in this question. He has the most direct AD-budget-approval experience from his Saint Vincent program-build work. Whatever proof point he names is the marketing brief.

SEGMENT-SPECIFIC DYNAMICS
====================================================

THE SOFT-SPLIT IS THE DIFFERENTIATOR

This is the only breakout running a structured tier order. The HS-first / college-second discipline matters for two reasons:

1. It prevents college coaches from anchoring the discussion before HS coaches have spoken. Coaches at higher tiers tend to dominate mixed conversations. The structure protects the HS read.

2. It produces parallel data points at synthesis. The HS-take and college-take on each question can be compared directly. The cross-tier divergence is the most valuable signal in the segment.

If the room drifts off the structure mid-question, gently redirect: "Let's hear from each of the HS coaches first, then we'll bring it to the college coaches."


THE FATHER-SON DYNAMIC WITH BUCK

Buck Larry coaches under his son Nolan as head coach. This means he holds a unique cross-lens: he has a coach perspective on parent-coach relationships AND a parent-coach perspective on athlete adoption. The parent layer is not directly on the worksheet, but if Buck's answers surface parent-side observations, that is D2C-grade signal coming from an Institutional seat. Tag that in your notes.
