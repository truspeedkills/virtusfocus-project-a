SECTION 1: THE PROBLEM
Speaker: Nicole | ~395 words | ~4 minutes
====================================================

Mental performance is a gap that everybody knows about and
nobody knows how to close at scale.

Every coach in this room knows the gap. Rick, Bret, and Bob
have coached at high levels for decades between them.

[LOOK AT EACH AS NAMED]

You have watched athletes whose mental game was the limiting
factor, not their physical capability. The athletes who
understood themselves were the ones who got the most out of
themselves.

The way mental coaching currently works is one of two ways.
One: a one-on-one relationship with a sport psychologist.
That works, when it works, but it is slow, expensive,
reactive, and usually only the most resourced athletes get
to access it. Two: a generic team meeting once a quarter
with a deck and a guest speaker, no follow-through, no
individual signal, no durability beyond the room.

Neither of those scales. Neither of those is durable.
Neither of those gives a coach insight into how an athlete
is actually carrying their week.

[PAUSE. TONE SHIFT TO DELIBERATE]

I want to draw a line right here, because if I don't,
somebody in this room is going to wonder about it for the
next three hours. There is mental performance work, and
there is mental health care.

[PAUSE]

They are not the same thing.

Mental health is clinical. Anxiety, depression, diagnosis,
treatment. It belongs to licensed professionals. It is
provided by campus counseling, by clinicians, by mental
health services. It is confidential clinical care.

Mental performance is another thing entirely. Focus under
pressure. Consistency. Confidence stability. How an athlete
responds after a mistake. Self-regulation. Leadership and
accountability. That is what "VirtusFocus" is. It is not
therapy and we are not pretending to be. When clinical care
is needed, it is handled by licensed professionals, exactly
as it should be.

We bake that distinction into every layer of what we built.
It matters for compliance coordinators. It matters for
legal teams. It matters for ADs. And it matters for parents.
We take it seriously, and we built the system around the
Performance vs Health distinction from the start.

[PACE PICKS BACK UP. TRANSITION INTO HAND-OFF]

Which gets us to what Arron has actually developed to close
that gap, while staying inside that line.

[TURN TO ARRON]

Arron.

SECTION 2: THE VISION
Speaker: Arron | ~720 words | ~7 minutes
====================================================

Thanks Nicole.

[BEAT]

Ok.

What we built is an athlete-led coaching system. The app is
the surface, the part you'll see in twenty minutes when I
walk you through the mockups. The system underneath the app
is where the actual work happens.

Here's what an athlete does daily. About five minutes,
total. In the morning they do something we call a Morning
Tune-Up. It's brief, it's structured, it sets an intention
for the day. In the evening they do a Review. Five
yes-or-no questions, brief journaling across three life
domains, and a Bullseye exercise that maps what they can
control, what they can influence, and what they can't.

Five minutes a day. Once a week, they do an eight to
twelve-minute weekly recap that goes deeper.

What that produces over the course of a week are hundreds
of signals that we can use to write a coaching message for
that athlete that lands like I have been with them every
day.

[PAUSE]

That's the bar.

For each athlete, every week, the system produces four
layers of output.

First, for the athlete: a coaching message and a Deep Dive
analysis. The message is direct, in my voice, about that
athlete specifically. The Deep Dive goes alongside it for
athletes who want to read deeper into their week.

Second, for the parents, in the D2C-Premium tier: a
parent-facing summary and parent-facing analytics that
respect the athlete's privacy. Parents see what they need
to support their kid. They do not see their kid's journal
entries, they do not see the raw daily inputs, they do not
get surveillance of the athlete. The athlete's reflective
space stays private.

Third, for institutional coaching staff: a weekly
performance dashboard for each athlete on the roster, plus
a team-level snapshot with athlete and team analytics
aggregated. Position coaches see what they need. Head
coaches see what they need. ADs see what they need. The
clinical line is held inside that dashboard the same way
it's held everywhere else: no clinical content, no
surveillance framing, no language that anybody on the staff
would have to redact.

Fourth, the daily layer that connects the other three. A
short morning coaching snippet that goes to the athlete
alongside their Morning Tune-Up. A daily coaching signal
that goes to the institutional coaching staff alongside
their dashboard. Daily cadence, kept short, kept on the
coaching side of the line.

Every layer is non-clinical. Every layer is rooted in the
athlete's own words.

[PACE SHIFTS. MORE DELIBERATE FOR THE NEXT BLOCK]

The hard part of building this, the thing nobody talks
about when they say "AI coaching," is that AI by default
produces generic output. Patterns leak in. Telegraphed
language. Phrases that sound like a coach who has not been
in the room. The voice flattens out and you end up with
something that reads more like a performance review than a
coaching conversation.

We built an audit layer behind every output that catches
and corrects those failure modes before anything reaches
the athlete. Every week, every athlete, every output gets
audited against fourteen specific criteria for voice
quality, clinical compliance, framework integrity, and
pipeline data leakage. If it fails, it gets corrected or
regenerated. The audit log is separate from the coaching
content. The athlete never sees the failures, they see the
corrected output.

The voice that comes out the other end is mine. The system
has spent about a hundred and twenty working sessions
teaching itself my voice, from real coaching transcripts
and athlete-by-athlete production output that I review and
revise. We monitor for drift weekly. When the voice slips,
we catch it and rebuild from real exemplars.

[PAUSE]

It is not chasing perfection. It is protecting the human
signal.

[PACE LIFTS. TRANSITION INTO PROOF]

In about eight minutes, when we move to the mockup
walkthrough, you'll see what this looks like as an app.
But before we get there I want to ground all of this in
three real athletes, because the whole point of the system
is the athletes who are using it. Three athletes I've
coached personally in Strength & Conditioning for over 5
years and over the last twelve weeks in this program.

Three completely different sports. Three completely
different stories. Same coach, same voice, different work.

[BEAT]

Athlete A first.

SECTION 3: PROOF (THREE ANCHOR STORIES)
Speaker: Arron | ~1,055 words | ~10 minutes
====================================================

Athlete A is a Division-Two women's softball player.
Outfielder. She's been in the program for twelve weeks now.
Her packet is in your materials and the twelve-week visual
is one of the artifacts in front of you, anonymized.

When she came into the program, the goal she wrote on her
own intake form was that she wanted to stay confident in
her ability during competition. She wrote that her
confidence depended on whether she got on the field. If
she played, she felt good. If she didn't, she felt
invisible.

She's an underclassman in a deep program. Playing time is
scarce. Her coach hasn't told her she's first off the
bench in weeks. Most athletes in her shoes either check
out or pretend it doesn't hurt. She did neither.

Twelve weeks in, her last full week, two things happened
that I want to read to you from her own writing.

[PACE SLOWS. THIS IS THE LOAD-BEARING BLOCK]

On Wednesday this past week, after practice, she wrote in
her recap:

[READ AS WRITTEN]

"fast asf today tbh, got all my mf bunts down."

[PAUSE]

That sentence.

[PAUSE]

That was the first time in twelve weeks she had named the
quality of her own work, in real time, without me asking
the question. The whole program has been teaching her to
do that. To stop waiting for the field to confirm her, and
to recognize what she's actually doing while she's doing
it. Twelve weeks of work to produce that one sentence. It
showed up Wednesday.

Then on Sunday, the same week, she wrote this. And I'm
going to read it exactly the way she wrote it.

[READ EXACTLY AS WRITTEN. DO NOT CORRECT GRAMMAR OR
SPELLING. THE CREDIBILITY IS IN THE VERBATIM.]

"I did so good, and it still just like doesnt matter. but i
cant say it doesnt matter because like it does, its just
annoying but once again oh well."

[LONG PAUSE]

That one sentence holds the entire week in one place. The
work was visible to her. She named it. The coaching staff
saw it. The outcome did not change. She did not get
playing time. And she kept going anyway.

That last part, kept going anyway, is what twelve weeks of
program have been building toward. When she walked in, her
confidence depended on the field. By Sunday of week
twelve, her confidence had moved out of needing the field
to confirm it. That is outcome-independent confidence, and
it is exactly what this program was built to produce.

[PAUSE. DELIBERATE EMPHASIS]

I did not write that for her. The system did not produce
that. She produced that, and the system caught it and
named it back to her at the right moment.

[PAUSE]

Her packet, with the twelve-week arc, is in your
materials.

[PACE LIFTS. TRANSITION TO ATHLETE B]

Athlete B is different. Division-Two men's track and
field. Throws. Shot put, discus, hammer. Senior, last year
of his collegiate career. Ten weeks in the program with
us.

Week four of his program, he competed at his conference
championship. He had a bad meet. He went home and
processed it. The next competitive week, week five, he
competed at the same events at a different meet, and he
broke his school record in shot put. Outdoor, school
record, the outdoor conference opener.

In his own words, after that meet:

[READ AS WRITTEN]

"It wasn't about hitting my furthest marks, just hitting
the marks I aimed for, while also setting myself up for a
good start."

That is a coaching philosophy in a single sentence,
written by the athlete. He stopped chasing outcomes and
started executing targets.

The why he gave for it was: "properly planned, not
spontaneous." That was his diagnosis of what went wrong at
the championship two weeks earlier, and what he had fixed
in the days between meets.

Then over the next five weeks, that preparation principle
expanded across three different timescales. Week four was
retrospective: looking backward at what failed and
extracting the lesson. Weeks five and nine were
prospective: planning forward into the next meet with
deliberate intention. Week nine, by the way, he broke the
school record again, this time at a different venue, while
his team had no consistent practice access and he had
skipped his usual lift schedule. The preparation system
held under worse conditions than the first time it had
worked.

And then week ten, the last regular season meet of his
collegiate career, the principle showed up at a third
timescale. Mid-competition. He was at a non-essential meet
on a wet day, the hammer ring was slippery, and he made a
real-time strategic decision to scratch the hammer event
to preserve himself for the conference championship the
following week. That decision was not spontaneous either.
He named it explicitly in his recap, the same way he had
named the lesson at week five.

[PAUSE]

A preparation principle that operates retrospectively,
prospectively, and in-the-moment. By his last week of his
collegiate career.

He advanced to the next program phase that week, his final
week. The system caught the advancement. He earned it.

[PACE LIFTS. TRANSITION TO ATHLETE C]

Athlete C is a high school wrestler. Brand new athlete,
first week back in the program after a season-ending knee
injury sustained in the state semifinals. Forfeited his
remaining matches, finished sixth in the state, surgery,
rehabilitation. He's still in an immobilizer and using
crutches.

Same week he started in our program, his team held its
end-of-season banquet. He won his school's Wrestler of
the Year award.

His first week back. His own weekly goal, written by him:

[READ AS WRITTEN]

"1% better everyday with the intention of healing as best
as possible."

The hard part of coaching a recovering athlete is that
there is no competition to anchor pre-performance routines
to. He's not training or wrestling. He's doing PT,
compression therapy, leg extensions, sunlight, family
meals, the rest days that are actually rest. There's no
opponent. There's no outcome to score against.

What he did, on his own, in week one, was take the
work-anchored thinking he had named on his intake form,
the kind of thinking that competitive athletes use before
high-stakes moments, and translate it directly into
recovery. Same hinge habit, different room. The system
noticed it and built the next coaching message on top of
it.

[PAUSE. WRAP THE THREE STORIES]

Three athletes. Three sports. Three completely different
weeks. Same coach. Same voice. Different work.

[PAUSE]

That is what scale looks like when the human signal stays
intact.

[TURN TO NICOLE]

And so this is where we are, and what we need from this
room.

[BEAT]

Nicole.

CONTINGENCY NOTES
=================

If running long (over 30 min):
  Trim Section 4 cross-room dynamics paragraph. The
  segment-specific asks are non-negotiable; the cross-room
  recognition is a nice-to-have that can compress to one
  sentence ("Several of you bring more than one
  perspective into your seat. We will ask about both in
  your breakouts.") if needed.

If running short (under 27 min):
  Add ~30-60 seconds to Athlete C (Hunter) by extending
  the work-anchored translation paragraph. Specifically,
  the moment where he translated competitive
  pre-performance thinking into recovery is the strongest
  beat in that story and can hold more weight.

If a savvy attendee asks "but who actually wrote the
coaching message, was it AI or you?" during Q&A
(after pitch, during breakouts, or at synthesis):
  Suggested answer for Arron: "I built the system. The
  system wrote the message. The athlete wrote the moment
  that produced both. The coaching signal is hers. The
  voice is mine. The audit layer makes sure the voice
  stays mine and the coaching stays clean."

If asked about pricing specifics in front of GTM segment:
  "Around twenty dollars a month for D2C-Premium" is what
  is committed in this script. Institutional pricing not
  named here intentionally; that comes up in the
  pricing/deployment block at 2:15-2:45.

If asked about clinical-vs-performance line in front of
institutional segment:
  Reinforce Section 1 framing. Mental health = clinical,
  licensed professionals. Mental performance = VirtusFocus,
  non-clinical, performance language only. Distinction is
  baked into every pipeline layer with explicit Stage 5
  audit criteria for clinical language detection.
